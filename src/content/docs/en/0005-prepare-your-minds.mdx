  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 1:13–16 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">13</sup>Therefore, preparing your minds for action, and being sober-minded, set your hope fully on the grace that will be brought to you at the revelation of Jesus Christ.
    <sup class="vnum">14</sup>As obedient children, do not be conformed to the passions of your former ignorance,
    <sup class="vnum">15</sup>but as he who called you is holy, you also be holy in all your conduct,
    <sup class="vnum">16</sup>since it is written, "You shall be holy, for I am holy."
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">For twelve verses Peter has only given us indicatives, statements of what God has done. Now comes the first imperative, and the word that opens it is <strong>"Therefore."</strong> That little word is the hinge of the Christian life. Everything Peter has built in verses 3 to 12 (mercy, new birth, living hope, an inheritance kept, a faith refined) now presses forward into commands. Grace never ends in a sigh of relief. It ends in a life reshaped. Notice where the reshaping begins: not with the hands, not with the mouth, but with the mind.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> The mind girded for action

  <p>The first command is a picture: <strong>"preparing your minds for action"</strong> (1:13). The literal image behind the Greek is older and earthier, literally "girding up the loins of your mind." In the ancient world a man wore a long robe, and to run or to work or to fight he had to gather the loose cloth and tuck it into his belt. Loose cloth trips you. Peter says: do that to your mind. Gather up the trailing, distracted, lazy thoughts, and tie them down (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 1:13</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
Holiness begins with <em>thinking</em>, not feeling. The first arena of Christian obedience is the mind. A scattered mind cannot carry a holy life for long. Before Peter says a word about conduct, he says: gather your thoughts.
</Aside>

  <p>Alongside it, <strong>"being sober-minded."</strong> The word (nēphontes) means clear-headed, free from intoxication, whether by wine or by fantasy or by fear. A sober mind is an undrugged mind, awake to reality. The <a href="/en/glossary/">sober-minded</a> believer sees both the trial and the hope without distortion. David Guzik is right that Peter's order matters: "hope that is fixed on grace" needs a mind that is both gathered and clear (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-1/">Guzik on 1 Pet 1:13</a>).</p>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> Hope set fully on grace

  <p>The main verb of verse 13 is in the middle: <strong>"set your hope fully on the grace that will be brought to you at the revelation of Jesus Christ."</strong> Three things deserve attention.</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Fully</strong> (teleiōs): not partly on grace and partly on something else. Hope divided is hope diluted. Peter wants our hope undivided.</li>
    <li><strong>On grace</strong>: not on our progress, not on the approval of people, not on circumstances turning out. On grace. The same ground that saved us is the ground we set our hope on now.</li>
    <li><strong>Brought to you at the revelation</strong>: hope faces forward, to the day Christ is unveiled. We hope for a Person, and for the grace He brings in His hand when He comes.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>So the first command is a hope command, before it is a holiness command. Adrian Rogers captures the sequence: <em>fix your hope on the grace to come, and the life will follow</em> (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 1:13</a>). Peter does not start with "be better." He starts with "set your hope fully."</p>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> Not conformed to former passions

  <p>Verse 14 turns to what we leave: <strong>"As obedient children, do not be conformed to the passions of your former ignorance."</strong> Notice the family language: <em>obedient children</em> (literally "children of obedience"). Peter does not say "behave like slaves." He says, you are <em>children</em>, and children of a holy Father carry the family likeness. Obedience is not hired work; it is the reflex of a child.</p>

  <p>And the command is negative first: <em>do not be conformed</em>. The word (syschēmatizomenoi) is the same root Paul uses in Romans 12:2, "do not be conformed to this world." It means to be poured into a mold, to let an external pattern press you into its shape. Peter warns against the old mold: <strong>"the passions of your former ignorance"</strong> (1:14). Before they knew God, these believers lived from appetites they did not understand. That was the old shape. The new shape is different.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
Sin is not just bad behavior; it is the residue of an old ignorance, the pattern of a life that did not know God. Holiness is not just effort; it is letting the new family shape replace the old mold, thought by thought, choice by choice.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">4.</span> Holy, because He is holy

  <p>Now the heart of the passage: <strong>"but as he who called you is holy, you also be holy in all your conduct, since it is written, 'You shall be holy, for I am holy'"</strong> (1:15-16). Peter quotes Leviticus (19:2; 11:44-45; 20:7), where God gave the same command to Israel at Sinai. Now he lays it on the church. The standard has not dropped. The God who called Israel is the God who has called us, and He has not changed.</p>

  <p>Notice the logic. Holiness is not first a standard to meet; it is a <em>Person to resemble</em>. <strong>"As he who called you is holy, you also be holy."</strong> The <a href="/en/glossary/">holy</a> God reproduces His own likeness in His children. Holiness is the family resemblance. And the scope is total: <strong>"in all your conduct"</strong> (anastrophē), the whole visible way of life, at the dinner table and at work and in private. Nothing is exempt.</p>

  <p>This is also where the priesthood through-line sounds again. The command "be holy, for I am holy" was first given to the people God was making a kingdom of priests (Ex 19:5-6). Peter, writing to the new priesthood, reaches back and takes the same calling onto the church. A holy priesthood serves a holy God.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
Grace has made us children of a holy Father, and the first thing His children do is gather their minds, set their hope fully on the grace to come, refuse the old mold, and take up the family likeness: holy, in all of conduct, because He is holy.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Take one ordinary part of your conduct this week, the way you speak at meals, the way you handle money, the way you react when delayed, and ask the single question Peter puts here: <em>does this look like my Father?</em> Let one small corner of your conduct be deliberately reshaped to the family likeness this week.
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that holiness is rooted in who God is and who He has made you, not in self-improvement. The standard is His character, and the power is the grace you hope for. Grace and holiness are not rivals; grace is the soil holiness grows from.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the affection of an obedient child, not the dread of a hired servant. Mortify the old passions that were formed in ignorance, and the quiet pride that thinks it can manage sin without a gathered mind and a fixed hope.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Gird up your mind. Pick one trailing, lazy thought pattern and tie it down this week. Set your hope fully on grace, not partly, and let one area of your conduct visibly bear the family likeness of the holy God who called you.</p>
