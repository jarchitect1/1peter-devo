  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 1:6–12">
<sup class="vnum">6</sup>In this you rejoice, though now for a little while, if necessary, you have been grieved by various trials,
    <sup class="vnum">7</sup>so that the tested genuineness of your faith, more precious than gold that perishes though it is tested by fire, may be found to result in praise and glory and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ.
    <sup class="vnum">8</sup>Though you have not seen him, you love him. Though you do not now see him, you believe in him and rejoice with joy that is inexpressible and filled with glory,
    <sup class="vnum">9</sup>obtaining the outcome of your faith, the salvation of your souls.
    <sup class="vnum">10</sup>Concerning this salvation, the prophets who prophesied about the grace that was to be yours searched and inquired carefully,
    <sup class="vnum">11</sup>inquiring what person or time the Spirit of Christ in them was indicating when he predicted the sufferings of Christ and the subsequent glories.
    <sup class="vnum">12</sup>It was revealed to them that they were serving not themselves but you, in the things that have now been announced to you through those who preached the good news to you by the Holy Spirit sent from heaven, things into which angels long to look.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">In verse 3 Peter gave us a living hope. In verse 6 he shows us where that hope has to live: inside a fire. The same breath that blesses God for mercy now admits that the mercied people <em>grieve</em>. Peter will not pretend the Christian life is painless. What he does is set the pain inside a purpose so large that it changes how the pain is carried. Read this passage slowly. It is one of the deepest passages on suffering and joy in the whole New Testament, and it is written for ordinary believers under ordinary pressure.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> Rejoicing and grieving at once</h2>

  <p>The first words are a surprise: <strong>"In this you rejoice, though now for a little while, if necessary, you have been grieved by various trials"</strong> (1:6). Joy and grief are not arranged in sequence, first one then the other. They coexist. The believers <em>rejoice</em> in the living hope of verses 3 to 5, and they <em>grieve</em> in the various trials of verse 6, at the same time.</p>

  <p>This is enormously freeing. Some teachings imply that real faith feels no grief, that any sadness is a failure of trust. Peter says otherwise. Grief is not the opposite of faith; it is the native habitat of faith in a fallen world. What matters is what the grief is doing.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The trials are <em>"various"</em> (poikilois, "many-colored"). Peter does not minimize them by pretending they are all the same. Each believer's fire looks different: a hard boss, a hostile family member, a slanderous neighbor, a weakening body. The grace is that the same hope covers every color of trial.
</Aside>

  <p>And note the two brackets around the grief: <strong>"for a little while"</strong> and <strong>"if necessary."</strong> The trial is short, measured against eternity. And it is necessary, measured against God's purpose. Both brackets are a kindness. The pain has a ceiling (<em>little while</em>) and a reason (<em>necessary</em>). Neither is obvious when you are inside the fire, which is exactly why Peter writes them down.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> Faith refined like gold</h2>

  <p>Now the purpose: <strong>"so that the tested genuineness of your faith, more precious than gold that perishes though it is tested by fire, may be found to result in praise and glory and honor at the revelation of Jesus Christ"</strong> (1:7). The picture is the refiner's fire. Gold is purified by heat; the impurities rise and are skimmed, and what remains is proven genuine. Peter borrows the image and then bends it. Faith, he says, is <em>more precious than gold</em>, and the gold perishes but the faith does not (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 1:7</a>).</p>

  <p>What does the fire prove? Not whether faith <em>exists</em>, but whether it is <em>genuine</em> (dokimion). Fire does not create faith; it reveals it. The trial strips away what was only profession and leaves what was true. Adrian Rogers puts it vividly: the trial "does not weaken faith, it reveals faith," and the faith that comes through the fire is the faith that will be praised at Christ's appearing (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 1:7</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
Your trial is not evidence that God has forgotten you. It may be the very instrument by which He is proving the reality of what He began in you. The fire is not the enemy of your faith; it is the friend of its genuineness.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> Loving the unseen Christ</h2>

  <p>Then Peter rises to one of the most tender verses in the letter: <strong>"Though you have not seen him, you love him. Though you do not now see him, you believe in him and rejoice with joy that is inexpressible and filled with glory"</strong> (1:8). These Gentile believers had never walked Galilee. They had no memory of His face. And yet they loved Him. That is the signature mark of the new birth: love for a Christ we have not seen.</p>

  <p>Notice how Peter piles up the verbs of the heart, and none of them depend on sight: <em>love, believe, rejoice</em>. And the joy is <em>inexpressible and filled with glory</em>. This is not the forced grin of positive thinking. It is a joy too large for words, shot through with the glory of the world to come, and it is felt <em>inside</em> the trial of verse 6, not after it. Joy and grief share the same verse.</p>

  <p>Verse 9 names the destination of this faith: <strong>"obtaining the outcome of your faith, the salvation of your souls."</strong> Faith is traveling somewhere. It is obtaining, even now by promise, a salvation that will be fully ours at the end. The trial does not interrupt that journey; it is part of how we travel it.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">4.</span> Prophets and angels, longing to look</h2>

  <p>In verses 10 to 12 Peter widens the lens to show these believers how large their salvation is. The Old Testament prophets <strong>"searched and inquired carefully"</strong> (1:10) about the very grace these believers now possessed. The Spirit of Christ in them was pointing to <strong>"the sufferings of Christ and the subsequent glories"</strong> (1:11). That pair, sufferings then glories, is the blueprint of this whole letter. Christ's road went through a cross to a crown. So does the believer's.</p>

  <p>And then the staggering line: it was revealed to the prophets that they <strong>"were serving not themselves but you"</strong> (1:12). The prophets longed to see what these ordinary, slandered Gentile believers already held. And if that were not enough, <strong>"things into which angels long to look"</strong> (1:12). The Greek for "long to look" (epithymeō parakyptō) paints angels stooping, craning to peer into the grace you currently carry (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-1/">Guzik on 1 Pet 1:12</a>; cf. <a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 1:12</a>).</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
Your grief is real, but it is not random. It is a refiner's fire proving a faith that prophets predicted, that angels envy, and that will end in praise and glory and honor at Christ's appearing. The fire is short. The faith is precious. The salvation is sure.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Take a current trial and write it in the center of a page. Around it, write the four brackets Peter gives: <em>little while, necessary, proving faith, ending in praise</em>. Pray the page over your trial once a day this week. Let the brackets hold what feels unbounded.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application: head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that your trials are neither pointless nor endless. They are <em>necessary</em>, <em>brief</em>, and <em>purposeful</em>, ordained to prove the genuineness of a faith more precious than gold. Sufferings then glories is the Christ-shaped road.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate love for the unseen Christ and a joy that does not wait for the trial to end. Mortify the lie that grief is a failure of faith, and the pride that thinks you deserve an easier fire than the prophets pointed to.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> When the next trial comes, refuse to read it as God's absence. Speak verse 7 over it: <em>this is proving my faith</em>. And let your joy and your grief be honest together, the way Peter lets them be honest in the same verse.</p>
