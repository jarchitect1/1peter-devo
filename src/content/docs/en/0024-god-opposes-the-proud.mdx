  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 5:6 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">6</sup>Humble yourselves, therefore, under the mighty hand of God so that at the proper time he may exalt you,
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">The last verse told us to <em>clothe</em> ourselves with humility, the outward apron toward one another. This verse goes one layer deeper: <em>humble yourselves</em> under the hand of God. It is one thing to wear the apron in the room; it is another to bow the knee under the divine hand. Both are required. The priesthood that is humble horizontally, toward one another, must also be humble vertically, under God. And this verse is a single sentence with a command and a promise: go down, and at the right time, He will lift you up.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> Humble <em>yourselves</em>

  <p>The command is active and intentional: <strong>"Humble yourselves, therefore, under the mighty hand of God"</strong> (5:6). Notice the grammar carefully. Peter does not say "be humbled," as if humility were something done to you passively. He says <em>humble yourselves</em> (tapeinōthēte). Humility, in the New Testament, is something you <em>do</em>, a choice to go low, a deliberate lowering of yourself under the hand of God. It is not a temperament; it is an act of the will, repeated.</p>

  <p>This is a searching point. We often wait to <em>feel</em> humble, or we imagine humility is a disposition God will somehow work in us. Peter will not allow the passivity. Humble <em>yourself</em>. Choose the low place. Take the seat at the foot of the table. Confess the sin first. Yield the preference. Apologize without being asked. The humble person is the one who has decided to go down, and who keeps deciding. Adrian Rogers draws the line: humility is not thinking poorly of yourself, it is simply not thinking of yourself <em>much at all</em>, because your eye is on God (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 5:6</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
There is a humility God works in us (cf. Phil 2:13) and a humility He commands us to work. Peter's emphasis here is on the commanded humility, the one you choose. The believer who waits for God to make him humble will wait a long time. The believer who obeys the command to go low finds that God meets him in the going down.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> Under the mighty hand of God

  <p>The object of the humility is arresting: <strong>"under the mighty hand of God"</strong> (5:6). The phrase "the mighty hand of God" is an Old Testament idiom for God's delivering power (Ex 13:9; Deut 3:24), the hand that brought Israel out of Egypt. Peter uses it here to mean God's sovereign, governing hand, the hand that is over all circumstances, including the suffering of these believers. To humble yourself under that hand is to bow before God's sovereign arrangement of your life, including the parts you did not choose.</p>

  <p>This is the secret of humility in hardship. The proud man chafes against his circumstances because he thinks he deserves better and could run things better. The humble man bows under the mighty hand, trusting that the God who holds all things has arranged even this. The same hand that crushed Pharaoh is the hand over your trial. You can go low under it, because the hand is mighty <em>and</em> good. Matthew Henry draws the comfort: the hand that humbles us is the hand that will exalt us, and it is mighty enough to do both (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 5:6</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
Humility under the mighty hand is the freedom of a man who has stopped trying to run the universe. You are not God, and the hand over your life is. Bowing under it is not defeat; it is relief. The proud carry a burden they were never made to carry. The humble set it down.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> At the proper time, He will exalt you

  <p>Now the promise that fuels the command: <strong>"so that at the proper time he may exalt you"</strong> (5:6). The going down is not the end of the story. The God who opposes the proud also exalts the humble, but He does it <em>at the proper time</em> (en kairō), the right time, His time, not ours. The humble believer does not get to choose when the lifting comes. He goes low and leaves the calendar to God.</p>

  <p>Notice how this reorders our ambition. The world's way is to exalt yourself now, by grasping, maneuvering, self-promoting. God's way is to humble yourself now and let Him exalt you in His time. Jesus said the same: "whoever humbles himself will be exalted" (Matt 23:12). The exaltation may come in this life (a restored role, an unexpected honour, a rebuilt reputation) or in the life to come, but it is as sure as the promise. David Guzik notes the relief of leaving the timing to God: the humble person is free from the anxiety of self-promotion because God has taken over the calendar (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-5/">Guzik on 1 Pet 5:6</a>).</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
Humble yourself, deliberately, under the mighty hand of God. The hand over your life is sovereign and good, and bowing under it is not defeat but relief. The lifting will come, at God's proper time, not yours. Go down now; He will raise you up then.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Name one circumstance you have been chafing against, trying to manage, or resenting. This week, deliberately "go down" under it: confess the pride that wanted to run it, and pray, "Your hand, Your time." Leave the calendar to God, and let the lifting come when and how He chooses.
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that the hand over your life is God's mighty, sovereign hand, that humility is something you choose rather than wait to feel, and that God's exaltation comes at His proper time, not yours. The proud carry a burden they were never made to carry.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the willingness to go low, under God's hand and under your circumstances. Mortify the pride that chafes against what God has arranged, and the self-promotion that wants to be exalted now, on your schedule.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Choose one act of deliberate self-lowering this week: a confession, a yielding, an apology, an acceptance of a circumstance you did not choose. Pray, "Your hand, Your time." Leave the lifting to God.</p>
