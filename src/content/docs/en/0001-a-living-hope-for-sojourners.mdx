  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 2:9–10">
<sup class="vnum">9</sup>But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession, that you may proclaim the excellencies of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light.
    <sup class="vnum">10</sup>Once you were not a people, but now you are God's people; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">Before we walk through this letter verse by verse, we need to see it whole. First Peter is a letter written to people who felt out of place, believers scattered across the northern edge of the empire, slandered by their neighbors, and anxious under a suffering that was only beginning. Into that, Peter does not first give them a strategy. He gives them an identity. Everything he will say for five chapters flows out of two realities: God has given them a <strong>living hope</strong> in the risen Christ, and He has made them His own <strong>priesthood</strong>. Hold those two in view, and the whole letter opens.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> Who wrote, and to whom</h2>

  <p>The letter opens with a name we know: <strong>Peter, an apostle of Jesus Christ</strong> (1:1). The early church received it as his without dispute, and Petrine authorship remains the standard evangelical position. He wrote through <strong>Silvanus</strong> (Silas), his faithful co-worker and the same Silas who served with Paul (5:12; cf. Acts 15–18). Peter is, in a sense, dictating this letter to a quiet bridge between the Petrine and Pauline circles.</p>

  <p>He writes from <strong>"Babylon"</strong> (5:13), almost certainly a symbolic name for <strong>Rome</strong>, the way Revelation uses it (Rev 17–18). The date is likely <strong>AD 62–64</strong>, just before the Neronian persecution broke over the church in full force. He is writing into a rising storm.</p>

  <p>The recipients are <strong>"the elect exiles of the Dispersion"</strong> scattered across Pontus, Galatia, Cappadocia, Asia, and Bithynia, the Roman provinces of northern Asia Minor (1:1). The letter shows them to be largely <strong>Gentile believers</strong> (1:14, 18; 2:9–10, 25; 4:3–4), people who once "were not a people" and are now, by mercy, God's people. They are new converts living as a small, suspect minority in towns that did not understand them. Peter's word for them becomes the letter's signature: <a href="/en/glossary/">sojourners, exiles, resident aliens</a>.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
Peter's first word for these believers is not "saints" or "church" but <em>exiles</em>. Before he tells them how to behave, he tells them where they live: not at home. The Christian's address is a pilgrimage.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> The occasion: slander, trial, and a steady hope</h2>

  <p>Why write at all? These believers were facing <strong>social hostility</strong>. Their neighbors were "speaking against" them as evildoers (2:12); they were being "maligned" for the name (4:4, 14, 16). The suffering was real, though it had not yet reached the level of empire-wide, state-sponsored execution. Peter writes to steady them. He will not pretend the trial away. He will give it <a href="/en/glossary/">meaning</a>, by setting it inside the purpose and pattern of Christ.</p>

  <p>His pastoral aim runs through the whole letter: <strong>encourage believers facing social hostility and slander, calling them to holy living, hopeful endurance, and humble witness under unjust suffering</strong>. That is the heartbeat of every chapter.</p>

  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 1:3–5">
<sup class="vnum">3</sup>Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! According to his great mercy, he has caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead,
    <sup class="vnum">4</sup>to an inheritance that is imperishable, undefiled, and unfading, kept in heaven for you,
    <sup class="vnum">5</sup>who by God's power are being guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time.
</Scripture>

  <p>Notice how the letter's opening doxology (the passage above) already contains its two great themes. The believers have a <strong>living hope</strong>, an inheritance kept for them in heaven, and they themselves are <strong>being guarded</strong> by God's power. The grammar is double-edged: the inheritance is kept <em>for</em> them, and they are kept <em>for</em> it. Hope and security together, before a single command is given.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> The map: five chapters, one argument</h2>

  <p>Keep the <a href="/en/1-peter-at-a-glance/">at-a-glance map</a> open as you study; it is the compressed version of what follows. Here is the simplest skeleton of the letter's argument:</p>

  <table>
    <thead><tr><th>Chapter</th><th>Center of gravity</th></tr></thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td>Chapter 1<br/><span class="small">A living hope for exiles</span></td><td>Born again to a living hope; be holy as God is holy; love one another earnestly, born of imperishable seed.</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Chapter 2<br/><span class="small">A royal priesthood</span></td><td>Living stones built into a spiritual house; a chosen race and <strong>royal priesthood</strong>. As sojourners, live such good lives that the slander is silenced; Christ left us an example.</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Chapter 3<br/><span class="small">Witness in the closest places</span></td><td>Husbands and wives as heirs of grace; unity and blessing in the fellowship; suffering for righteousness with Christ's victory proclaimed.</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Chapter 4<br/><span class="small">Living for God in the fire</span></td><td>Arm with Christ's mindset; love and serve with the gifts received; do not be surprised at the fiery trial; glorify God in it; entrust your soul to a faithful Creator.</td></tr>
      <tr><td>Chapter 5<br/><span class="small">Shepherd, humble, watch</span></td><td>Elders shepherd willingly and eagerly, as examples; all clothe yourselves with humility; cast anxiety on Him; resist the devil, firm in the faith.</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>

  <p>The movement is unmistakable. Peter begins with <strong>identity</strong> (who we are in Christ), and from that flows <strong>life</strong> (how we then live). He never reverses the order. Holiness, submission, suffering, humility, and watchfulness are never duties heaped onto an exhausted believer; they are the natural breathing of a people who have already been born again into a living hope.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">4.</span> The through-line: the priesthood of all believers</h2>

  <p>This series has one thread we will trace in every lesson: <strong>the priesthood of all believers</strong> (2:9). Peter draws the phrase straight from Exodus 19:5–6, where God first promised Israel, "you shall be to me a kingdom of priests and a holy nation." Now he applies it to the church, Jew and Gentile together. Every believer is a priest: <strong>direct access to God through Christ</strong>, and a <strong>calling to offer spiritual sacrifices and proclaim God's excellencies</strong>. There is no separate priestly caste mediating between the believer and God.</p>

  <p>For an assembly with brethren convictions, this is familiar ground, and it is where Peter himself plants his flag. Read the thesis verse again (2:9, quoted at the top of this lesson) and notice the chain: <em>chosen race → royal priesthood → holy nation → God's possession → "that you may proclaim"</em>. The identity exists <em>for</em> the mission. We are priests in order to proclaim.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The series in one breath">
Because God has given us a living hope in the risen Christ and made us His chosen, holy priesthood, we live as faithful sojourners, holy in conduct, submissive in our stations, joyful in suffering, humble in the fellowship, and watchful to the end.
</KeyIdea>

  <p>Every chapter of 1 Peter, and so every lesson of this series, is one facet of that single sentence. When you lose your bearings in a difficult passage, come back to it.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">5.</span> How to read this letter devotionally</h2>

  <p>First Peter is not a doctrinal textbook to be mastered, nor a sermon outline to be preached. It is a <strong>shepherd's letter</strong>, written by a man who had failed, been restored, and knew suffering firsthand (cf. Luke 22; John 21). Read it the way you would read a letter from a father who has walked the road ahead of you. Let the commands land softly on the hope that has already been announced. Let the identity feed the conduct, never the reverse.</p>

  <p>As we go, three small habits will serve you well:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Read with the at-a-glance map open.</strong> You will always know where you are in the whole.</li>
    <li><strong>Let the vocabulary settle in.</strong> Words like <em>sojourner</em>, <em>living hope</em>, <em>royal priesthood</em>, and <em>suffering</em> are load-bearing. The <a href="/en/glossary/">glossary</a> is there for re-reading.</li>
    <li><strong>Ask, "who are we, and therefore how do we live?"</strong> That is the question Peter keeps answering.</li>
  </ul>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Before the next lesson, read the whole of 1 Peter aloud, in one sitting, if you can. It takes about fifteen minutes. Do not study yet; just listen for the two voices underneath everything: <em>you have a living hope</em>, and <em>you are a priesthood</em>. Mark where you hear each one. That is the seed of everything to come.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application: head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that 1 Peter is, above all, a letter of <em>identity before conduct</em>. God has made us His chosen priesthood with a living hope, and our obedience flows from that, not toward it.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the affection of a <em>sojourner</em>: at home in heaven, resident but not at home on earth. Mortify the craving for the world's approval that would make the slander sting more than it should.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> One concrete step this week: read the letter through once, aloud if possible. Carry the question "who are we, and therefore how do we live?" into your reading, and bring one observation to your next conversation with a brother or sister in the assembly.</p>
