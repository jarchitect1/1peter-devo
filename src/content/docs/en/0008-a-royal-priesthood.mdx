  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 2:1–10">
<sup class="vnum">1</sup>So put away all malice and all deceit and hypocrisy and envy and all slander.
    <sup class="vnum">2</sup>Like newborn infants, long for the pure spiritual milk, that by it you may grow up into salvation,
    <sup class="vnum">3</sup>if indeed you have tasted that the Lord is good.
    <sup class="vnum">4</sup>As you come to him, a living stone rejected by men but in the sight of God chosen and precious,
    <sup class="vnum">5</sup>you yourselves like living stones are being built up as a spiritual house, to be a holy priesthood, to offer spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ.
    <sup class="vnum">6</sup>For it stands in Scripture: "Behold, I am laying in Zion a stone, a cornerstone chosen and precious, and whoever believes in him will not be put to shame."
    <sup class="vnum">7</sup>So the honor is for you who believe, but for those who do not believe, "The stone that the builders rejected has become the cornerstone,"
    <sup class="vnum">8</sup>and "A stone of stumbling, and a rock of offense." They stumble because they disobey the word, as they were destined to do.
    <sup class="vnum">9</sup>But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession, that you may proclaim the excellencies of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light.
    <sup class="vnum">10</sup>Once you were not a people, but now you are God's people; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">This is the passage the whole series has been walking toward. The through-line, the <a href="/glossary/">royal priesthood</a>, is named here. But notice where Peter puts it: not at the start of the letter as a slogan, but in the middle of a passage about laying down sin, longing for milk, and being built onto a Stone that some people trip over. The priesthood is not a status we strut; it is a temple we are <em>built into</em>, and the building begins with repentance and appetite. Read this slowly. The famous verses 9 and 10 are the destination of verses 1 through 8, not a detached motto.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> Lay it down, and long</h2>

  <p>The temple passage opens, surprisingly, with a command to <em>take off</em>: <strong>"So put away all malice and all deceit and hypocrisy and envy and all slander"</strong> (2:1). The word is the language of taking off a dirty garment. Before we are built up, we are stripped down. And notice the list: malice (the wish to harm), deceit (the lie that gets your way), hypocrisy (the mask), envy (the resentment of another's good), slander (the mouth that wounds). All of them are <em>relational</em> sins. They break the brotherhood. You cannot build a living temple with lying, envying stones.</p>

  <p>Then the positive command, and it is an appetite: <strong>"Like newborn infants, long for the pure spiritual milk, that by it you may grow up into salvation"</strong> (2:2). Peter does not say <em>study</em> the milk or <em>agree to</em> the milk. He says <em>long for</em> it, the way a hungry infant does, without pretense, without delay. The "pure spiritual milk" is the word of God (cf. 1:23-25), the gospel in which we tasted that the Lord is good (2:3, echoing Ps 34:8). Adrian Rogers catches the picture: a Christian without appetite for the word is a sick Christian; the healthy soul is hungry (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 2:2</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
Growth into our salvation (2:2) is paired with laying aside the sins that break the brotherhood (2:1). A believer who feeds on the word but keeps slandering, and a believer who stops slandering but never feeds, are both stunted. Peter wants both: appetite <em>and</em> honesty.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> Coming to the living Stone</h2>

  <p>Then the picture that frames the whole passage: <strong>"As you come to him, a living stone rejected by men but in the sight of God chosen and precious"</strong> (2:4). Christ is the Stone, and He is <em>living</em>, the same word used for our hope in 1:3. He was rejected by people (the cross) but chosen and precious in God's sight (the resurrection and exaltation). What people throw away, God builds on.</p>

  <p>And then the astonishing identification: <strong>"you yourselves like living stones are being built up as a spiritual house"</strong> (2:5). We are not visitors to the temple; we <em>are</em> the temple. Each believer a stone, fitted alongside the others, built on the Cornerstone. Notice the grammar: it is passive and continuous, <em>being built up</em>. We do not build ourselves; we <em>are being built</em>. And we are built <em>together</em>, not alone. A lone stone is not a temple. The priesthood is corporate before it is individual (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 2:5</a>).</p>

  <p>And to what end? <strong>"To be a holy priesthood, to offer spiritual sacrifices acceptable to God through Jesus Christ"</strong> (2:5). Here the priesthood through-line sounds for the first time as a direct identity. We are the <a href="/glossary/">holy priesthood</a>. Our sacrifices are no longer animals but <em>spiritual</em>: the praise of our lips, the doing of good, the sharing of what we have (cf. Heb 13:15-16). And they are acceptable <em>through Jesus Christ</em>, never on their own.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> The Stone that saves or stumbles</h2>

  <p>Now Peter strings together three Old Testament quotes about a stone, and they split humanity into two. First, from Isaiah 28: <strong>"Behold, I am laying in Zion a stone, a cornerstone chosen and precious, and whoever believes in him will not be put to shame"</strong> (2:6). To the one who believes, the Stone is honor and security. Then from Psalm 118: <strong>"The stone that the builders rejected has become the cornerstone"</strong> (2:7). The very One people discarded has been made the foundation. And third, from Isaiah 8: <strong>"A stone of stumbling, and a rock of offense"</strong> (2:8). To the one who disobeys, the same Stone is a trip-hazard.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
Christ is the same Stone to everyone. What differs is how a person meets Him. To the believer, He is the chosen, precious foundation. To the one who disobeys the word, He is the stone they stumble over. You cannot be neutral about this Stone. You either build on Him or trip over Him.
</Aside>

  <p>Verse 8 ends with a sober line: <strong>"They stumble because they disobey the word, as they were destined to do."</strong> The stumbling is just, because it flows from disobedience. David Guzik is careful here: the destiny of the stumbling lies in the nature of the disobedient response to the word, not in a capricious decree (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-2/">Guzik on 1 Pet 2:8</a>). The same Stone saves the trusting and crushes the proud.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">4.</span> The fourfold identity, and the purpose</h2>

  <p>Now the summit of the letter: <strong>"But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a people for his own possession"</strong> (2:9). Each phrase is drawn from Exodus 19:5-6 and Isaiah 43, where God named Israel His own at Sinai. Peter takes the covenant language of the old people of God and lays it, without apology, on a largely Gentile church. The church does not replace Israel as a political nation; the church <em>shares in</em> the same covenant identity, grafted into the one people of God through Christ (cf. Rom 11; Eph 2:11-22).</p>

  <p>But note the purpose clause that completes the verse, because the identity is never an end in itself: <strong>"that you may proclaim the excellencies of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light"</strong> (2:9). The priesthood exists to <em>proclaim</em>. We were given an identity in order to advertise the God who gave it. "Excellencies" (aretas) are the virtues, the praiseworthy deeds, of God. The priest's job is to make God look as good as He is.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
You are a living stone, built with others into a spiritual house, made a royal priesthood, for one purpose: to proclaim, by word and life, the excellencies of the God who called you out of darkness into His light. The priesthood is the identity; proclamation is the job.
</KeyIdea>

  <h2><span class="num">5.</span> Once you were not a people</h2>

  <p>The passage ends in mercy: <strong>"Once you were not a people, but now you are God's people; once you had not received mercy, but now you have received mercy"</strong> (2:10). Twice the past, twice the now. Whatever we were (scattered, not-a-people, unmercied), we are now God's gathered, mercied people. Matthew Henry lingers on the contrast: the greatest dignity of the believer is mercy received, not status earned (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 2:10</a>). The priesthood never outgrows its origin in mercy. We proclaim His excellencies because we were once not a people, and now we are.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Read verse 9 aloud, slowly, inserting your own name: "But [your name] is part of a chosen race, a royal priesthood." Then answer, in one sentence this week: <em>What excellence of God am I, by my life this week, proclaiming to someone still in the dark?</em> Let the priesthood have a purpose under your roof.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application — head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that the church is the true temple, built on Christ the living Cornerstone, and that you are a living stone in it, sharing the covenant identity once spoken over Israel. You are a royal priest, not by ordination but by new birth.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate an appetite for the pure milk of the word and a reverence for the Cornerstone. Mortify the relational sins of verse 1, malice, deceit, hypocrisy, envy, slander, that would make you a crooked stone in the wall.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Offer spiritual sacrifices this week: praise, good done in His name, generosity, a quiet refusal to slander. And find one way, in word or deed, to proclaim an excellence of the God who called you out of darkness. The priest's job is proclamation.</p>
