    <h1>Suffering Is Not the Final Word</h1>
    <p class="passage">After a little while, the God of all grace will restore, confirm, strengthen, establish</p>
    <div class="lesson-no">Lesson 27 · 1 Peter 5:10–11</div>
  </div>

  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 5:10–11 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">10</sup>And after you have suffered a little while, the God of all grace, who has called you to his eternal glory in Christ, will himself restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish you.
    <sup class="vnum">11</sup>To him be the dominion forever and ever. Amen.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">This passage is the pastoral promise Peter has been building toward for five chapters. He has told us to arm our minds, to rejoice in fiery trials, to entrust our souls, to humble ourselves, to cast our anxieties, to resist the lion. And now he tells us how the story ends. After a little while of suffering, the God of all grace Himself will do four things to His battered people, four verbs of complete restoration. The lion is not the final word. The fire is not the final word. The suffering is not the final word. The God of all grace is the final word, and His word is restoration. Then a doxology seals it.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> After a little while</h2>

  <p>The promise is bounded by time: <strong>"And after you have suffered a little while"</strong> (5:10). Notice the same bracket Peter has been putting around suffering since 1:6, the trial is <em>for a little while</em>. However long the suffering feels, it has a ceiling. Eternity will dwarf it. Paul said the same: "this light momentary affliction is preparing for us an eternal weight of glory" (2 Cor 4:17). The royal priest carries the fire knowing it is temporary, and that knowledge is itself a fuel.</p>

  <p>And notice the realism. Peter does not say <em>if</em> you have suffered; he says <em>after</em> you have suffered. The suffering is assumed. These are people in the fire, and Peter writes to them a promise of what comes <em>after</em>. The comfort is not "the fire will skip you"; the comfort is "the fire has a finish, and the finish is glory." Adrian Rogers draws the line: the believer's suffering is always "a little while," but his glory is forever (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 5:10</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The suffering is real and it is assumed, but it is bracketed. "A little while" is not minimization; it is perspective. The believer does not deny the fire; he puts a time-limit on it by faith. However long the fire burns in time, it will be a candle against the sunrise of eternity.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> The God of all grace</h2>

  <p>Now the One who acts: <strong>"the God of all grace, who has called you to his eternal glory in Christ"</strong> (5:10). Two staggering phrases. First, <em>the God of all grace</em> (pasēs charitos). Not some grace, not occasional grace, but <em>all</em> grace. God is the fountain of every kindness, and the restoration He brings is an outflow of His own gracious character, not our deserving. Second, the One who <em>called you to His eternal glory in Christ</em>. The calling is upward, into glory, and it is <em>eternal</em>. The suffering is momentary; the glory is forever.</p>

  <p>Notice the Chalcedonian precision of "in Christ." The calling to glory is <em>in Christ</em>, never apart from Him. The same Christ who suffered (5:1) is the Christ in whom we are called to glory. The road is one road: sufferings with Him now, glory with Him then. Matthew Henry lingers on the comfort: the God who calls us to glory is the God who will get us there, and His grace is equal to every step (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 5:10</a>).</p>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> Four verbs of complete restoration</h2>

  <p>Now the fourfold promise, and the cumulative effect is overwhelming: <strong>"will himself restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish you"</strong> (5:10). Notice first the word <em>himself</em> (autos). God does not delegate the restoration; He does it personally. The God of all grace is hands-on with His broken people. Then the four verbs, each a distinct angle of complete healing:</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Restore</strong> (katartisei). A mending word, the same word used for the disciples "mending" their nets (Matt 4:21). God will mend what the fire has torn. The broken parts will be fitted back together.</li>
    <li><strong>Confirm</strong> (stērizei). To set fast, to make stable. The believer shaken by the trial will be made unshakable. What suffering loosened, grace fastens.</li>
    <li><strong>Strengthen</strong> (sthenōsei). To give strength, to invigorate. The believer drained by the fight will be filled with new power, divine strength supplied.</li>
    <li><strong>Establish</strong> (themeliōsei). A foundation word, to set on a sure foundation. The believer will be placed on the rock, never to be moved again.</li>
  </ul>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
The fire does not have the last word over your soul. Whatever it has torn (God will mend), whatever it has shaken (God will set fast), whatever it has drained (God will strengthen), whatever it has loosened (God will establish). The God of all grace does not leave His people as the fire found them. He restores them, better than before.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">4.</span> The doxology of dominion</h2>

  <p>The passage ends, and the letter nearly ends, in praise: <strong>"To him be the dominion forever and ever. Amen"</strong> (5:11). Notice the word Peter ascribes to God: <em>dominion</em> (kratos), the might, the ruling power. The believer has just been told about the prowling lion (5:8), and now he is told about the dominion of God. The lion hunts; God rules. The lion is strong; God is <em>all</em>-strong, and His dominion is <em>forever and ever</em>. The doxology is the answer to the devil: whatever power the adversary seems to wield, it is bounded and temporary; the dominion belongs to God, eternally.</p>

  <p>And the "Amen" is the congregation's voice. The suffering church, hearing the promise, answers with the affirming <em>so be it</em>. David Guzik notes the placement: the doxology is the only fitting response to the fourfold promise, because a church that has heard of such restoration cannot stay silent (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-5/">Guzik on 1 Pet 5:11</a>). The royal priest who suffers opens his mouth not in complaint but in doxology.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
After a little while of suffering, the God of all grace, who called you to eternal glory in Christ, will Himself restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish you. The fire is temporary; the restoration is complete; the dominion is God's forever. The suffering priest ends in doxology, because suffering is not the final word.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Read verse 10 aloud, slowly, inserting yourself: "After <em>I</em> have suffered a little while, the God of all grace will <em>himself</em> restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish <em>me</em>." Let the four verbs land on whatever the fire has done to you. Then answer with verse 11: <em>To Him be the dominion forever and ever. Amen.</em>
</Aside>

  <h2>Application: head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that your suffering is bounded ("a little while"), that your calling is to eternal glory in Christ, and that the God of all grace will personally restore, confirm, strengthen, and establish you. The fire does not get the final word; God does.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the hope that looks past the fire to the restoration. Mortify the despair that reads suffering as permanent, and the complaint that reads it as pointless. The lion hunts, but God's dominion is forever, and that is the louder truth.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> End where the letter ends: in doxology. Whatever your current suffering, take verse 10 as a personal promise and verse 11 as your answer. Let the Amen be real. The royal priest carries the fire with a song on his mouth, because the God of all grace is the final word.</p>
