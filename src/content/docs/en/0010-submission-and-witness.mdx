  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 2:13–17">
<sup class="vnum">13</sup>Be subject for the Lord's sake to every human institution, whether it be to the emperor as supreme,
    <sup class="vnum">14</sup>or to governors as sent by him to punish those who do evil and to praise those who do good.
    <sup class="vnum">15</sup>For this is the will of God, that by doing good you should put to silence the ignorance of foolish people.
    <sup class="vnum">16</sup>Live as people who are free, not using your freedom as a cover-up for evil, but living as servants of God.
    <sup class="vnum">17</sup>Honor everyone. Love the brotherhood. Fear God. Honor the emperor.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">This is the first of Peter's four "stations" of submission: the civil authority (2:13-17), the workplace (2:18-25), the marriage (3:1-7), and the flock (5:5). Before we hear the word <em>submit</em> as a restriction, hear how Peter frames it: he addresses it to <em>free</em> people (2:16), and he grounds it <em>for the Lord's sake</em> (2:13). The royal priest is not crushed by authority; the royal priest <em>freely</em> orders himself under it, because his Lord did, and because the watching world is reading his conduct. Submission, in Peter's hands, is a witness strategy, not a surrender of dignity.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> "For the Lord's sake" changes everything</h2>

  <p>The command: <strong>"Be subject for the Lord's sake to every human institution"</strong> (2:13). The key phrase is the motive clause: <em>for the Lord's sake</em>. Peter does not say the emperor is good. The emperor, when Peter wrote, was likely Nero, the very regime about to turn its full cruelty on the church. Peter is not naive. He says the believer submits <em>not because the authority deserves it, but because the Lord is served by it</em>. The <a href="/en/glossary/">submission</a> (hypotassō) is rendered to the authority, but the offering is made <em>to Christ</em>.</p>

  <p>This is the brethren-assembly instinct, and the plain reading of the text: authority is God-given, even when the office-holder is wicked (cf. Rom 13:1; John 19:11). Adrian Rogers draws the line cleanly: we honor the office because God established the office, even when we grieve the man who holds it (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 2:13</a>). The believer's ultimate loyalty is never in question, and that is exactly why he can afford to submit to a secondary one.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
There is a limit Peter assumes rather than states: we submit "for the Lord's sake," which means the moment the authority commands what the Lord forbids, or forbids what the Lord commands, we obey the Lord (cf. Acts 5:29). Submission is real, but it is not absolute. Only God has absolute authority.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> The state as God's servant</h2>

  <p>Peter explains the structure: <strong>"to the emperor as supreme, or to governors as sent by him to punish those who do evil and to praise those who do good"</strong> (2:13-14). The state, in its proper function, is God's deacon. It exists to restrain evil and commend good. That is the design, even when the execution is broken. Paul says the same in Romans 13:3-4. The believer submits because, behind the human office, he sees the divine appointment.</p>

  <p>And then the immediate purpose: <strong>"For this is the will of God, that by doing good you should put to silence the ignorance of foolish people"</strong> (2:15). Notice how practical Peter is. The believers were being slandered as evildoers (2:12), as subversive, as a threat to the empire. The temptation would be to revolt, to withdraw, or to rail. Peter's answer is to <em>do good visibly</em> within the structures, so that the slander is silenced by the evidence. Submission is a testimony strategy.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> Free people, gladly serving</h2>

  <p>Now the verse that holds the whole passage in balance: <strong>"Live as people who are free, not using your freedom as a cover-up for evil, but living as servants of God"</strong> (2:16). The Christian is genuinely free, free from sin, free from the fear of man, free from the need to control. But that freedom is never a license. It is the freedom of a <em>servant of God</em>. We are free <em>from</em> sin in order to be free <em>for</em> service.</p>

  <p>This is the paradox at the heart of the priesthood. A priest is the freest person in the room (he answers ultimately to God alone), and the most bound (he belongs entirely to God). Matthew Henry captures the balance: Christian liberty is "a freedom from sin, not a freedom to sin," and the believer who uses grace as a cloak for rebellion has misunderstood his freedom entirely (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 2:16</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
You are free. You do not need to grasp, manipulate, or rebel to prove it. Your freedom is shown, not by throwing off authority, but by gladly ordering yourself under it as one who belongs to God. The freest person can afford to submit, because his security is not in the state but in the Lord.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">4.</span> Four commands, in scale</h2>

  <p>The passage ends with four short imperatives, and their order is a sermon in itself: <strong>"Honor everyone. Love the brotherhood. Fear God. Honor the emperor"</strong> (2:17). Notice the gradation of intensity, because not all relationships call for the same response.</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Honor everyone.</strong> Every person, believer or not, bears the image of God and is owed dignity. We honor the unbelieving neighbor, the difficult colleague, the stranger. Everyone.</li>
    <li><strong>Love the brotherhood.</strong> The family of God gets more than honor; it gets <em>love</em>, the earnest love of 1:22. The church is a deeper bond than nation or neighbor.</li>
    <li><strong>Fear God.</strong> Only God gets <em>fear</em>, the reverent awe of 1:17. The deepest response is reserved for Him alone.</li>
    <li><strong>Honor the emperor.</strong> And the emperor? He gets <em>honor</em>, the same word as "everyone." The state is owed real respect, but it is not owed worship. Only God is feared.</li>
  </ul>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
The free royal priest submits to civil authority, not because the authority is perfect, but for the Lord's sake, so that the slander is silenced by seen good. Honor everyone, love the brotherhood, fear God, and honor the emperor. The scale matters: only God is feared.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Take verse 17 and pray it over four actual people or groups this week: one unbelieving neighbor or official ("honor everyone"), one believer you must love ("love the brotherhood"), God Himself ("fear God"), and one civil authority you find hard to respect ("honor the emperor"). Let the four commands reorder your actual relationships.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application: head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that civil authority is God-ordained in its design, that submission to it is rendered "for the Lord's sake," and that your freedom in Christ is a freedom for service, not a cover for rebellion. Only God's authority is absolute.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the secure humility of a free person who can afford to honor others. Mortify the pride that despises authority, and the fear that treats the state as ultimate. Honor is not the same as worship.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Pick one civil obligation you are tempted to resent (a tax, a law, an official) and this week render it "for the Lord's sake," cheerfully and visibly. And practice verse 17 in scale: honor broadly, love the brethren deeply, fear God alone.</p>
