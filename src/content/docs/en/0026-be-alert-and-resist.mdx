  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 5:8–9 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">8</sup>Be sober-minded; be watchful. Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion, seeking someone to devour.
    <sup class="vnum">9</sup>Resist him, firm in your faith, knowing that the same kinds of suffering are being experienced by your brotherhood throughout the world.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">The royal priesthood is not only a serving and humble fellowship; it is a <em>warring</em> fellowship. Peter has just told us to humble ourselves and cast our anxieties on God. Now he tells us why we must stay alert: there is an adversary, actively hunting. The two commands are paired on purpose. The humblest priest is the most alert priest, because he knows he cannot afford to drift. These two verses give the priesthood its end-times posture: sober, watchful, resisting, and never alone. The lion is real, but he is not invincible, and the flock that resists him firm in the faith will not be devoured.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> Sober-minded and watchful

  <p>Two commands open the passage, and they are the <a href="/en/glossary/">watchfulness</a> pair that has run through the letter: <strong>"Be sober-minded; be watchful"</strong> (5:8). We met these in 1:13 (the gathered mind for hope) and 4:7 (the clear head for prayer). Here they are the sentry's posture. Sober-minded (nēpsate) is the undrugged, undistracted mind; watchful (grēgorēsate) is the awake, attentive mind, the word Jesus used for His sleepy disciples in Gethsemane (Mark 14:38). Together they describe a head that is up and in hand.</p>

  <p>Notice that Peter does not treat the devil as an abstract theological idea. He treats him as a present threat that requires <em>active alertness</em>. The believer who is spiritually drowsy, distracted, or amused is exactly the believer the lion is looking for. Adrian Rogers draws the line bluntly: a careless Christian is a conquered Christian; the call to watchfulness is not optional equipment, it is basic survival (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 5:8</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The two halves of the priesthood's posture belong together: humility (5:5-7) and watchfulness (5:8). The humble priest casts his anxiety; the watchful priest keeps his eyes open. A humble but drowsy priest is an easy target. A watchful but proud priest has already fallen. Both garments are required.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> The prowling, devouring lion

  <p>Now the reason for the alertness, in a stark image: <strong>"Your adversary the devil prowls around like a roaring lion, seeking someone to devour"</strong> (5:8). Four truths in one sentence. First, he is <em>your adversary</em> (antidikos), a legal term for an opponent in a lawsuit. He has a case against you. Second, he is <em>the devil</em> (diabolos), the slanderer, the one who accuses. Third, he <em>prowls</em>, walks about, restless, never idle, looking for the gap. Fourth, his aim is to <em>devour</em> (katapiein), to swallow down, to consume entirely. He is not looking for a small win; he is looking for the whole believer.</p>

  <p>The lion image is carefully chosen. A roaring lion in Peter's world, in the arena and in the wild, was a picture of terror. The devil hunts like a lion that has already fed and is roaring in triumph and threat, or like one driving prey with its roar. Either way, the picture is of a real, dangerous, intentional predator. The royal priest does not romanticize the devil, and he does not dismiss him. He takes him seriously enough to stay awake (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 5:8</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
The devil is not a metaphor. He is an active adversary, hunting for the careless, the isolated, the proud, the drowsy. The priest's safety begins with taking him seriously. The believer who keeps his head up and his mind sober is the believer the lion finds harder to reach.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> Resist him, firm in the faith

  <p>Now the command that answers the lion: <strong>"Resist him, firm in your faith"</strong> (5:9). The verb "resist" (antistēte) means to stand against, to take a stand. It is a military word, the soldier planting his feet. And notice <em>how</em> the resistance is done: <em>firm in your faith</em>. The believer does not resist the devil by cleverness, by argument, or by sheer will. He resists by <em>standing in what he believes</em>, the fixed, settled faith once for all delivered to the saints (cf. Jude 3). The shield that quenches the flaming darts is faith (Eph 6:16).</p>

  <p>This is the same pattern James gives: "Resist the devil, and he will flee from you" (Jas 4:7). The resistance is not a dramatic confrontation; it is the stubborn refusal to move off the ground of the gospel. When the devil lies, stand on the truth. When he accuses, stand on the blood (1:2). When he tempts to despair, stand on the hope (1:3). David Guzik notes that "firm" (stereoi) means solid, immovable; the faith is not a feeling to be worked up but a foundation to be stood on (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-5/">Guzik on 1 Pet 5:9</a>).</p>

  ## <span class="num">4.</span> Never alone: a worldwide brotherhood

  <p>And the closing encouragement, easily missed: <strong>"knowing that the same kinds of suffering are being experienced by your brotherhood throughout the world"</strong> (5:9). The lion wants you to think you are alone, isolated, uniquely targeted, the only one struggling. Peter says you are not. The same sufferings are being experienced by the <em>brotherhood</em>, the whole worldwide family, throughout the world. The attack is not a private misfortune; it is the shared experience of the church universal.</p>

  <p>This is deeply comforting and deeply steadying. The devil's strategy of isolation is defeated by the knowledge of solidarity. You are not singled out; you are one of millions, across the world and across history, who have stood in the same faith and faced the same lion. And the lion has never once devoured the church. Matthew Henry draws the courage: the knowledge that we are not alone in the fight is itself a weapon; the brotherhood is the context of the resistance (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 5:9</a>).</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
Be sober and watchful: your adversary the devil hunts like a roaring lion for someone to devour. Resist him, not by cleverness but by standing firm in the faith, and remember you are not alone, the same sufferings are shared by the brotherhood throughout the world. The humble, alert, resisting priest is not devoured.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Name one area where the lion is currently prowling (a recurring temptation, an accusing thought, a despairing lie). Then identify the specific truth of the faith that resists it. When the lie comes, plant your feet on the truth and say it aloud. And remind yourself: <em>I am not the only one in this fight.</em>
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that the devil is a real adversary who hunts for the careless, that resistance is done by standing firm in the faith, and that you are not alone in the fight. The brotherhood throughout the world faces the same lion.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the sober watchfulness of a sentry. Mortify the drowsiness that amuses itself to sleep, the pride that thinks it could never fall, and the despair that whispers you are uniquely singled out. The lie of isolation is one of the lion's oldest tools.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Resist by standing on the truth. When the lie comes, plant your feet on the specific promise that answers it. And refuse isolation: stay close to the brotherhood, share the fight, and remember the millions who stand with you in the same faith worldwide.</p>
