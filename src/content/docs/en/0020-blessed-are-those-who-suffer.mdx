  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 4:14–16 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">14</sup>If you are insulted for the name of Christ, you are blessed, because the Spirit of glory and of God rests upon you.
    <sup class="vnum">15</sup>But let none of you suffer as a murderer or a thief or an evildoer or as a meddler.
    <sup class="vnum">16</sup>Yet if anyone suffers as a Christian, let him not be ashamed, but let him glorify God in that name.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">The last lesson told us not to be surprised by the fiery trial and to rejoice in the sharing of Christ's sufferings. This passage presses the same truth further, in three careful moves. First, a blessing pronounced over the insulted Christian, with a staggering reason attached. Second, a pointed exclusion: there is a kind of suffering you must <em>not</em> be experiencing, and Peter names it bluntly. Third, the right response to the right suffering: do not be ashamed; glorify God in the name. The royal priest under slander carries a blessing, refuses the wrong suffering, and turns reproach into worship.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> The blessing on the insulted, with its reason

  <p>The opening condition: <strong>"If you are insulted for the name of Christ, you are blessed"</strong> (4:14). The word "insulted" (oneidizēsthe) means reproached, mocked, derided. Jesus used the same word in the Beatitudes: "Blessed are you when others revile you... on my account" (Matt 5:11). And here, as there, the blessing is not a consolation prize; it is a present reality. <em>You are blessed.</em> Not you will be; you <em>are</em>, even now, in the very moment of the insult.</p>

  <p>Then the breathtaking reason: <strong>"because the Spirit of glory and of God rests upon you"</strong> (4:14). Read that slowly. The Spirit who rested on Christ at His baptism, the Spirit of glory, rests on the believer when he is insulted for the name. The reproach that the world means for shame is, in the Spirit's economy, the occasion of glory. The same Presence that anointed Jesus rests on the insulted Christian. Adrian Rogers draws the wonder: where the world sees shame, the Spirit sees glory, and He settles there (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 4:14</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The Spirit rests, present tense, on the insulted believer. The reproach for Christ is not a sign that God has withdrawn; it is, mysteriously, the setting in which the Spirit of glory comes to rest. The path of suffering is the path of the Spirit's most manifest presence.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> The suffering you must not be doing

  <p>Now a sharp exclusion, and it shows Peter's honesty about the human heart: <strong>"But let none of you suffer as a murderer or a thief or an evildoer or as a meddler"</strong> (4:15). Peter knows that a suffering Christian can too easily play the martyr for sins he actually committed. He will not have it. There is no blessedness in being punished for wrongdoing.</p>

  <p>Notice the list. Murderer, thief, evildoer, these are obvious. Then the curveball: <em>or as a meddler</em> (allotriotriepiskopos), a wonderfully specific Greek word, literally "an overseer of other people's matters." The Christians in Asia Minor were apparently prone to poking into affairs that were not theirs, and then suffering for it as if they were being persecuted. Peter says that is not suffering for Christ. That is just being a busybody. David Guzik notes the warning still applies: a great deal of "persecution" Christians suffer is actually the natural consequence of being annoying (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-4/">Guzik on 1 Pet 4:15</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
Before you claim the blessing of 4:14, check the cause of your suffering. Is it really <em>for the name</em>, or is it for your own sin, your own sharp tongue, your own meddling? The priest must be honest here. Only suffering for righteousness carries the blessing; suffering for wrongdoing carries only its own shame.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> Do not be ashamed; glorify God in the name

  <p>Now the right response to the right suffering: <strong>"Yet if anyone suffers as a Christian, let him not be ashamed, but let him glorify God in that name"</strong> (4:16). Two commands, paired. First, <em>do not be ashamed</em>. The temptation under insult is to shrink, to mute the name, to pretend you are not one of them. Peter says no. The name "Christian" (christianos) was originally coined by outsiders, somewhat dismissively (Acts 11:26), but Peter wears it here with honour. To suffer <em>as a Christian</em> is no disgrace.</p>

  <p>Second, the positive: <em>glorify God in that name</em>. The insult that was meant to shame you becomes the occasion of worship. Turn the reproach upward. When they sneer at the name, praise God for the name. Matthew Henry draws the logic: the believer's suffering is not a thing to be hidden but a thing to be lifted up, because the name that draws the reproach is the name above every name (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 4:16</a>).</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
To be insulted for the name of Christ is to be blessed, because the Spirit of glory rests on you there. But beware the wrong suffering, do not earn your pain by sin or meddling. And when you suffer truly as a Christian, do not be ashamed; turn the reproach into worship and glorify God in the very name they mock.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Ask yourself honestly this week: <em>Have I been suffering for the name, or suffering for my own sharpness, my own meddling, my own faults?</em> If the latter, repent and stop. If the former, take 4:14 as your blessing and 4:16 as your response: do not hide the name, glorify God in it.
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that insult for Christ's name carries a present blessing, because the Spirit of glory rests on the insulted believer. Believe also that suffering for your own sin or meddling carries no blessing at all. The cause of the suffering matters.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the freedom from shame that lets you wear the name Christian without flinching. Mortify the martyr-complex that dresses up your own faults as persecution, and the cowardice that hides the name when it costs.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Stop any suffering you have been earning by wrongdoing or meddling. And when the true insult for the name comes, do not mute it; turn the moment into worship, and glorify God in the very name that drew the reproach.</p>
