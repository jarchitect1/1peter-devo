  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 3:13–17 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">13</sup>Now who is there to harm you if you are zealous for what is good?
    <sup class="vnum">14</sup>But even if you should suffer for righteousness' sake, you will be blessed. Have no fear of them, nor be troubled,
    <sup class="vnum">15</sup>but in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy, always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you; yet do it with gentleness and respect,
    <sup class="vnum">16</sup>having a good conscience, so that, when you are slandered, those who revile your good behavior in Christ may be put to shame.
    <sup class="vnum">17</sup>For it is better to suffer for doing good, if that should be God's will, than for doing evil.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">This is one of the most loved and most quoted passages on Christian witness, and it deserves to be read in its setting. Peter is still writing to slandered, anxious believers, people who may genuinely suffer for doing right. The famous "always being prepared to make a defense" (3:15) is not a stand-alone mandate for a debate club; it is a <em>suffering</em> passage. The hope we are to explain is the hope that keeps us unafraid when the cost of righteousness rises. Read verse 15 as the center of verses 13 to 17, not as a slogan. The apologetic flows from a heart that has first set Christ apart as Lord.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> A reassuring question, a real exception

  <p>Peter opens gently: <strong>"Now who is there to harm you if you are zealous for what is good?"</strong> (3:13). It is a rhetorical question, and the assumed answer is: by and large, no one. Most people, even unbelievers, do not punish you for doing good. A genuinely good life usually wins a measure of peace (cf. 2:14-15). So Peter steadies them first: usually, zeal for good is safe.</p>

  <p>But then the exception, the case the letter was written for: <strong>"But even if you should suffer for righteousness' sake, you will be blessed"</strong> (3:14). Notice the blessedness. Jesus said the same in the Beatitudes (Matt 5:10). Suffering <em>for righteousness</em> is not a sign of God's absence but a mark of His blessing. The world may take everything else; it cannot take the blessing of a Father who pronounces His favor on the faithful.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
There is a suffering that is blessed (for righteousness) and a suffering that is not (for doing evil, verse 17). Peter is very careful not to glamorize all pain. The suffering he calls blessed is the kind that comes <em>because</em> you did right, the same distinction he made in 2:20. Suffering well begins with suffering for the right reason.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> Do not fear; set Christ apart instead

  <p>Now two commands, one negative and one positive, drawn straight from Isaiah 8: <strong>"Have no fear of them, nor be troubled, but in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy"</strong> (3:14-15). The negative is a direct quote of Isaiah 8:12, do not fear what they fear. The positive is Isaiah 8:13, where the Lord is to be the One we <em>do</em> fear, in the reverent sense. The logic is: fear displaced is fear redirected. If you do not fear the persecutors, it is because you have given that fear to Christ, setting Him apart as Lord in the heart.</p>

  <p>The phrase <strong>"in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy"</strong> is the devotional core of the passage. The verb (hagiasate, "sanctify, set apart") means to give Christ the exclusive place of ultimate reverence <em>inside</em>, where only He belongs. When Christ is Lord in the heart, the loudest human threat is relativised. Adrian Rogers draws the line: a Christian who fears people more than he fears Christ has set the wrong thing apart as Lord (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 3:14-15</a>). Settle the Lordship inside, and the witness outside follows.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
The boldness of the believer's witness is not bravado; it is interior. It comes from a heart that has given Christ the only throne. When He is set apart as Lord <em>there</em>, the fear of what people can do loses its grip, and the mouth is free to speak of hope.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> Always prepared to give the reason

  <p>Now the famous command, flowing directly from the heart-Lordship: <strong>"always being prepared to make a defense to anyone who asks you for a reason for the hope that is in you"</strong> (3:15). The word "defense" (apologian) is a legal term for a reasoned answer, the root of "apologetics." But notice what we are defending: not a system, not a denomination, but <em>the hope that is in us</em>. People should be able to look at the Christian and see hope, so visible that they ask, <em>why?</em> The witness begins with a life of hope so real it draws questions.</p>

  <p>And notice the manner: <strong>"yet do it with gentleness and respect"</strong> (3:15). The very next breath qualifies the defense. Not with strut, not with contempt, not with the bitterness of one who has a score to settle. With gentleness (the same prautēs of 3:4) and respect (phobos, a reverent fear, likely toward God). The apologetic that wins the hearing is the one given humbly, by a person whose tone is already a witness. Matthew Henry warns: a truth spoken arrogantly has already contradicted itself (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 3:15</a>).</p>

  ## <span class="num">4.</span> A good conscience, a shamed slanderer

  <p>The witness is also protected by a clean life: <strong>"having a good conscience, so that, when you are slandered, those who revile your good behavior in Christ may be put to shame"</strong> (3:16). The strategy returns to 2:12, the slander answered by a seen life. When the accusation is false, the truth of the conduct eventually exposes it. A good conscience is not a cover for sin but the freedom of one whose life can bear inspection. David Guzik notes the protective force: a consistent life robs the slanderer of ammunition (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-3/">Guzik on 1 Pet 3:16</a>).</p>

  <p>And the closing principle that summarizes the whole: <strong>"For it is better to suffer for doing good, if that should be God's will, than for doing evil"</strong> (3:17). This is Peter's settled conviction. Even if God wills the suffering (and sometimes He does), it is better to suffer <em>for good</em> than to escape by doing evil. The royal priest accepts the cost rather than compromise the conduct.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
When suffering for righteousness comes, it is blessed. Do not fear it. Instead, set Christ apart as Lord in the heart, and out of that interior Lordship, be always ready to give, with gentleness and a clean conscience, the reason for the hope within you. The witness flows from the throne of the heart.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Write one sentence this week: <em>Here is the reason for the hope that is in me</em>, and finish it in your own words. Practice saying it aloud, gently, until it feels natural. You are not preparing a lecture; you are preparing the answer to the question a watching person may one day ask you.
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that suffering for righteousness is blessed, that fear of people is displaced by setting Christ apart as Lord in the heart, and that the believer's apologetic is a reason <em>for hope</em> given with humility, not a system defended with strut.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the interior Lordship of Christ that frees you from the fear of what people can do. Mortify the dread of their opinions, and the pride that would answer their questions with contempt rather than gentleness.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Be ready. Write your one-sentence reason for your hope. Speak it gently when asked. And keep your conscience clean, so that any slander against you has no true charge to grip. The witness is a life and a word together.</p>
