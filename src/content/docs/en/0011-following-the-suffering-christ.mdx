  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 2:18–25 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">18</sup>Servants, be subject to your masters with all respect, not only to the good and gentle but also to the unjust.
    <sup class="vnum">19</sup>For this is a gracious thing, when, mindful of God, one endures sorrows while suffering unjustly.
    <sup class="vnum">20</sup>For what credit is it if, when you sin and are beaten for it, you endure? But if when you do good and suffer for it you endure, this is a gracious thing in the sight of God.
    <sup class="vnum">21</sup>For to this you have been called, because Christ also suffered for you, leaving you an example, so that you might follow in his steps.
    <sup class="vnum">22</sup>He committed no sin, neither was deceit found in his mouth.
    <sup class="vnum">23</sup>When he was reviled, he did not revile in return; when he suffered, he did not threaten, but continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly.
    <sup class="vnum">24</sup>He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die to sin and live to righteousness. By his wounds you have been healed.
    <sup class="vnum">25</sup>For you were straying like sheep, but have now returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of your souls.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">This is the second of Peter's stations, the workplace, addressed to household servants. But the passage outruns its first audience. Anyone who has ever been treated unfairly by someone with power over them is in this text. And the heart of it is not a rule about employees; it is a portrait of Christ. Peter does two things at once here that we usually pull apart. He gives us Christ as our <em>example</em> (how to suffer), and Christ as our <em>substitute</em> (He suffered <em>for</em> us). The famous healing verse, "by his wounds you have been healed" (2:24), is the center of this passage, not a detached promise. Read it in its setting.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> Servants under unjust masters

  <p>The address: <strong>"Servants, be subject to your masters with all respect, not only to the good and gentle but also to the unjust"</strong> (2:18). The word "servants" (oiketai) means household servants, often slaves, the working people of the ancient world. Peter does not endorse the institution; he speaks Christ into it, to people who had no legal power to leave. (A note on application: the text dignifies labor and the worker, not slavery. The enduring principle is how a believer bears injustice from a person in authority, in any setting where they cannot simply walk away.)</p>

  <p>Notice the realism of "not only to the good and gentle but also to the unjust." Peter knows there are harsh masters, unreasonable bosses, cruel authorities. He does not pretend otherwise. And he does not tell the servant to revolt. He tells the servant to submit <em>with respect</em>, even when the authority is unjust. That is a hard word, and Peter knows it, which is why he spends the next seven verses explaining <em>why</em>.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The submission is "with all respect." Peter is not asking for cringing terror or groveling. He is asking for the inner posture of one who respects the God who sees, even when the human authority does not deserve it. The respect is rooted upward, which is why it can survive injustice downward.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> A gracious thing, mindful of God

  <p>Now the <em>why</em>: <strong>"For this is a gracious thing, when, mindful of God, one endures sorrows while suffering unjustly"</strong> (2:19). The phrase "a gracious thing" (charis) means a thing that finds favor with God, a thing that participates in grace. Enduring unjust suffering is not meaningless; it is, when borne "mindful of God," an act that God Himself honors.</p>

  <p>And Peter sharpens it in verse 20: there is no credit in taking a beating you deserved. <strong>"But if when you do good and suffer for it you endure, this is a gracious thing in the sight of God."</strong> The favor of God rests specifically on the suffering that is <em>undeserved</em>, the suffering that comes <em>because</em> the believer did right. That is the suffering that images Christ. That is the suffering Peter is writing about.</p>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> The example: His steps

  <p>Now the ground: <strong>"For to this you have been called, because Christ also suffered for you, leaving you an example, so that you might follow in his steps"</strong> (2:21). Two truths in one sentence. First, we are <em>called</em> to this. Undeserved suffering is not an interruption of the Christian life; in a fallen world that hates Christ, it is part of the calling. Second, Christ has gone before us and left an <em>example</em> (hypogrammon), a word for the pattern-letter a child traced to learn to write. Christ's path is the template we trace, step by step.</p>

  <p>Then Peter paints the example in four strokes from Isaiah 53:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>"He committed no sin, neither was deceit found in his mouth"</strong> (2:22). His suffering was utterly undeserved. He deserved none of it.</li>
    <li><strong>"When he was reviled, he did not revile in return"</strong> (2:23). He absorbed the insult without returning it. No retaliation.</li>
    <li><strong>"When he suffered, he did not threaten"</strong> (2:23). He did not pull rank, did not warn of what He could do. Silence.</li>
    <li><strong>"He continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly"</strong> (2:23). Instead of threatening the abuser, He trusted the Judge. He handed His case to the Father.</li>
  </ul>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
The secret of bearing unjust suffering is in the last stroke: <em>entrusting</em>. Christ did not answer the reviler because He had handed the whole matter to the One who judges justly. When we do the same, we are freed from the curse of retaliation. We do not need to win this round, because the just Judge will settle all rounds.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">4.</span> The substitute: His wounds

  <p>Now the passage turns from <em>example</em> to <em>substitute</em>, and here is where 2:24 sits in its true home: <strong>"He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die to sin and live to righteousness. By his wounds you have been healed"</strong> (2:24). Adrian Rogers lingers here, as he should: Christ did not merely show us <em>how</em> to die; He died <em>for</em> us. The example would crush us without the substitute, because we could never trace it. The substitute makes the example possible, because He first bore what we could not (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 2:24</a>).</p>

  <p>Notice the purpose clause: <strong>"that we might die to sin and live to righteousness."</strong> The cross was not only to forgive us; it was to <em>change</em> us. He bore our sins so that sin would lose its grip on us and righteousness would become our life. And then the healing: <strong>"By his wounds you have been healed."</strong> Taken with verse 25 ("you were straying like sheep"), the healing is the cure for our wandering, the restoration of the sheep to the Shepherd. This is not a generic promise of physical healing ripped from context; it is the <em>substitutionary</em> healing of sin-sick souls by the wounded Shepherd (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 2:24</a>; cf. <a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-2/">Guzik on 1 Pet 2:24</a>).</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
When you are treated unjustly by one in authority, you are walking the path Christ walked. He left you an example (entrust yourself to the just Judge, do not retaliate), and He went before you as your substitute (He bore your sins so you could die to sin and live to righteousness). By His wounds you are healed; by His steps you follow.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
When you next feel the heat of unjust treatment, before you reply, say verse 23 to yourself: <em>He entrusted Himself to Him who judges justly.</em> Hand that specific matter to the just Judge in a single sentence of prayer. Then, freed from the need to retaliate, do good. That is tracing His steps.
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that Christ is both your example in unjust suffering and your substitute for sin. The example would crush you without the substitute; the substitute makes the example followable. By His wounds you are healed; His steps are the path.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the quiet trust of one who has handed his case to the just Judge. Mortify the reflex of retaliation, the rehearsing of grievances, and the threat-making that wants to prove you could win if you tried.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> The next time you are wronged by someone with power over you, do not revile in return, do not threaten. Entrust yourself to God in a breath-prayer, and then do good in return. Trace His steps in one concrete situation this week.</p>
