  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 5:12–14">
<sup class="vnum">12</sup>By Silvanus, a faithful brother as I regard him, I have written briefly to you, exhorting and declaring that this is the true grace of God. Stand firm in it.
    <sup class="vnum">13</sup>She who is at Babylon, who is likewise chosen, sends you greetings, and so does Mark, my son.
    <sup class="vnum">14</sup>Greet one another with the kiss of love.
    Peace to all of you who are in Christ.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">The letter ends as it began, with grace and peace. Five chapters of fire and hope, of identity and submission and suffering, close in three short verses of greeting. It is easy to skim an ending like this and miss that it carries the whole letter's weight in compressed form. Peter names his carrier, sums up his message in one sentence, sends the greetings of the church and of his son in the faith, commands a visible love, and pronounces a peace. Read these verses as the seal on everything that came before. The royal priesthood that has walked through fire is now sent back into its week with a final charge: <em>stand firm in the true grace of God.</em></p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> Silvanus, the faithful carrier</h2>

  <p>The first name is the one who carried the letter: <strong>"By Silvanus, a faithful brother as I regard him, I have written briefly to you"</strong> (5:12). This is <a href="/glossary/">Silvanus</a>, Silas, the same faithful co-worker who served with Paul (Acts 15:22, 40; 2 Cor 1:19; 1 Thess 1:1). Peter trusts him with the delivery of this letter across the long roads of Asia Minor, and he commends him as a "faithful brother." In the early church, the carrier of a letter was not a postman; he was also its interpreter, the one who read it aloud and explained its heart. Silas carries both the parchment and the pastoral weight.</p>

  <p>Notice Peter's modesty about the letter itself: <em>I have written briefly</em>. Five chapters of Scripture, and the author calls it brief. There is no inflation here. The apostle is not building a monument to his own words; he is sending a pastoral note to suffering people, and he wants the message, not the messenger, magnified. David Guzik notes the humility: Peter's "briefly" reminds us the letter is a servant of the grace it carries, not an end in itself (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-5/">Guzik on 1 Pet 5:12</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The letter was carried by a faithful brother and read aloud in the assembled churches. First Peter was not written for private study alone; it was written to be heard in the gathering, explained by the carrier, and obeyed together. The royal priesthood is a letters-to-be-read-together people.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> The whole letter in one sentence</h2>

  <p>Now Peter's own summary of everything he has written, and it is breathtaking in its compression: <strong>"exhorting and declaring that this is the true grace of God. Stand firm in it"</strong> (5:12). Two verbs. First, <em>exhorting</em> (parakalōn), the pastoral word of coming alongside and urging. Second, <em>declaring</em> (epimarturōn), bearing solemn witness, testifying. The letter is both an urge and a testimony. And the content of both is one thing: <em>this is the true grace of God</em>.</p>

  <p>Read that phrase as the lens for the whole letter. The living hope (1:3), the sprinkled blood (1:2), the royal priesthood (2:9), the suffering that shares Christ's (4:13), the restoration of 5:10, all of it is <em>the true grace of God</em>. Peter has been drawing a portrait of grace from fifty angles, and now he names it. And the command that seals it is only four words: <em>Stand firm in it.</em> Not in your circumstances, not in your feelings, not in your performance. In the grace. Adrian Rogers catches the final note: the Christian's stability is never in himself but in the grace he is told to stand in (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 5:12</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
The whole letter reduces to a charge: stand firm in the true grace of God. Whatever fire you are in, whatever lion you face, whatever anxiety you carry, the ground under your feet is grace, and the command is to stand in it. Do not slide off into performance. Do not collapse into despair. Stand in the grace.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> Babylon and Mark: a greeting across the miles</h2>

  <p>Then the greetings: <strong>"She who is at Babylon, who is likewise chosen, sends you greetings, and so does Mark, my son"</strong> (5:13). Two names deserve attention. First, <em>Babylon</em>. Almost certainly this is a symbolic name for <strong>Rome</strong>, the way Revelation uses it (Rev 17-18) and the way later Roman usage developed. Peter is writing from Rome, near the end of his life, and "she who is at Babylon... likewise chosen" is the church there, the sister congregation, sending greetings across the empire to the suffering churches of Asia Minor. (A minority read this as literal Babylon or as Jerusalem, but Rome is the standard evangelical position; cf. NOTES.md.)</p>

  <p>Second, <em>Mark, my son</em>. This is John Mark, the young man who once deserted the work (Acts 13:13) and caused a sharp parting between Paul and Barnabas (Acts 15:37-39), but who was later restored and useful (2 Tim 4:11). Peter calls him "my son," an affectionate term that reflects a deep spiritual fatherhood. Early tradition says Mark's Gospel preserves Peter's preaching. The restoration of Mark is one of the quiet mercies of the New Testament, and here he stands, named with honour, at the end of Peter's letter. Matthew Henry draws the comfort: the God who restored Peter and Mark is the God who restores the bruised reader of this letter (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 5:13</a>).</p>

  <h2><span class="num">4.</span> The kiss of love, and the final peace</h2>

  <p>Now two short commands that close the letter in warmth: <strong>"Greet one another with the kiss of love"</strong> (5:14). The kiss was the cultural greeting of family, and Peter commands it as the visible sign that the church is one family across every divide. It is to be a kiss of <em>love</em> (agapēs), not formality. The same earnest love commanded in 1:22 and 4:8 here gets a body. The royal priesthood greets each other as the family it is.</p>

  <p>And the very last word of the letter: <strong>"Peace to all of you who are in Christ"</strong> (5:14). The letter began with "grace and peace" (1:2); it ends with peace. <em>Peace</em> (eirēnē), the settled wholeness, the <em>shalom</em>, of being right with God. To believers about to suffer, Peter's last word is not warning but shalom. And it is peace <em>in Christ</em>, the only safe place, the location of every blessing the letter has named. All who are in Christ receive it.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
The letter closes as it opened, with grace and peace. Silvanus carried it; Peter summed it up in a charge: this is the true grace of God; stand firm in it. The sister church in Rome and the restored Mark send greetings. The priesthood greets one another with the kiss of love, and the last word over all who are in Christ is peace.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Take Peter's summary as your own standing order this week: <em>this is the true grace of God; stand firm in it.</em> When the week shakes you, do not stand in your circumstances or your performance. Stand in the grace. And, in some concrete way, greet a brother or sister in the assembly with a visible, warm love, the family love this letter has been commanding all along.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application — head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that the whole letter is a portrait of the true grace of God, and that your stability is in standing in that grace, not in your circumstances or your performance. The letter is brief because the grace is what matters.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the settled peace (shalom) of one who is in Christ. Mortify the slide into self-reliance or despair that would knock you off the ground of grace. The last word over you is peace, because the ground under you is grace.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Stand firm in the grace this week, by a deliberate refusal to slide off it when pressed. And greet the brethren with a real, warm, family love, the visible kiss of an invisible bond. The priesthood ends where it began, in grace and peace, together.</p>
