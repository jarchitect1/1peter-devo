  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 3:8–12 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">8</sup>Finally, all of you, have unity of mind, sympathy, brotherly love, a tender heart, and a humble mind.
    <sup class="vnum">9</sup>Do not repay evil for evil or reviling for reviling, but on the contrary, bless, for to this you were called, that you may obtain a blessing.
    <sup class="vnum">10</sup>For "Whoever desires to love life and see good days, let him keep his tongue from evil and his lips from speaking deceit;
    <sup class="vnum">11</sup>let him turn away from evil and do good; let him seek peace and pursue it.
    <sup class="vnum">12</sup>For the eyes of the Lord are on the righteous, and his ears are open to their prayer. But the face of the Lord is against those who do evil."
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">Peter has spent three chapters shaping the believer's life toward the outside world (the state, the workplace, the watching neighbors, the unbelieving spouse). Now, with the word <em>Finally</em>, he turns to the <em>inside</em> of the church. The royal priesthood is first a fellowship, and the health of its inner life is the condition of its outward witness. A church that bites itself cannot bless the world. So Peter gathers five virtues, one calling, and one Psalm, and asks: when the assembly is together, what should it feel like? It should feel like blessing.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> Five virtues of the fellowship

  <p>The list is compact and luminous: <strong>"Finally, all of you, have unity of mind, sympathy, brotherly love, a tender heart, and a humble mind"</strong> (3:8). Notice that Peter addresses "all of you," because these are not the gifts of a few leaders but the shared disposition of every member. In a brethren assembly, where every priest ministers, this is the air the whole room breathes.</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Unity of mind</strong> (homophrones). Not identical opinions on everything, but a shared mind, the same foundational allegiance to Christ that holds differences without fracture.</li>
    <li><strong>Sympathy</strong> (sympatheis). To feel <em>with</em>. The brother's joy is mine, the sister's grief is mine. No cold distance in the body.</li>
    <li><strong>Brotherly love</strong> (philadelphoi). The warm family affection of 1:22. The church is not an organization we join; it is a family we are born into.</li>
    <li><strong>A tender heart</strong> (eusplanchnoi). Literally "good bowels," the seat of compassion in the ancient idiom. A heart that moves toward the other's need, not away from it.</li>
    <li><strong>A humble mind</strong> (tapeinophrones). The <a href="/en/glossary/">humility</a> that does not need to win, does not need to be seen, does not need to be first.</li>
  </ul>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
Three of the five are about the <em>mind</em> (unity of mind, humble mind) and the <em>heart</em> (tender heart, sympathy). The fellowship Peter wants is not built by programs but by inner dispositions. A roomful of tender, humble, like-minded people is already a witness before anyone says a word.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> The calling: to bless

  <p>Then the command that reframes everything: <strong>"Do not repay evil for evil or reviling for reviling, but on the contrary, bless, for to this you were called, that you may obtain a blessing"</strong> (3:9). The natural reflex when wronged is to return it: evil for evil, insult for insult. Peter says no. The royal priest blesses instead.</p>

  <p>Notice the weight of the motive: <em>to this you were called</em>. Blessing the one who wrongs us is not a higher option for the spiritual; it is the calling. And it is also the path to our own blessing: <em>that you may obtain a blessing</em>. The God who blessed us calls us to be a blessing, and the very act of blessing is the way we inherit more of the blessing ourselves (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 3:9</a>). Adrian Rogers catches the logic: the Christian is a walking blessing, because he has been blessed, and the blessing flows through him rather than stopping on him (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 3:9</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
The priest is a conduit, not a reservoir. The blessing of God is meant to flow through him to the very person who wronged him. When we return a curse for a curse, we dam up the blessing; when we bless instead, the river keeps flowing, and we are watered in the flowing.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> The Psalm of the good life

  <p>To ground it, Peter quotes Psalm 34 (the same Psalm he drew on in 2:3): <strong>"Whoever desires to love life and see good days, let him keep his tongue from evil and his lips from speaking deceit; let him turn away from evil and do good; let him seek peace and pursue it"</strong> (3:10-11). Peter is writing to sufferers, and he tells them, in the words of David, that the good life is found <em>not</em> in avenging yourself but in the disciplined tongue, the turned-away-from-evil life, and the pursued peace.</p>

  <p>Notice the three actions in the quote. First, the <em>tongue</em>: the discipline of blessing starts in the mouth (cf. James 3). Second, the <em>turning</em>: a concrete refusal of evil and a choice of good. Third, the <em>pursuit</em> of peace: peace is not passive, something we fall into; it is something we chase. "Seek peace and pursue it" is active, energetic, the believer going after harmony as a hunter goes after game (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-3/">Guzik on 1 Pet 3:11</a>).</p>

  ## <span class="num">4.</span> The Lord's face, for and against

  <p>The passage ends with a sobering pair: <strong>"For the eyes of the Lord are on the righteous, and his ears are open to their prayer. But the face of the Lord is against those who do evil"</strong> (3:12). The same God who watches over the righteous with attentive eyes and open ears sets His face against the one who does evil. Peter is warning the church, do not suppose the inner life of the assembly is a small thing. The God who hears our prayers is also the God who turns His face from the one who repays evil for evil.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
The royal priesthood is a blessing people. We share one mind, feel with one another, love as family, keep tender hearts, and walk in humility. And when wronged, we bless instead of retaliating, because to this we were called. The God whose eyes are on the righteous waters the blessing we give away.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Identify one person in the assembly (or beyond it) who has wronged you, even slightly, and whom you are tempted to repay in kind. This week, <em>bless</em> them instead, in a word of gratitude, a prayer kept, a kindness done. Trace the calling: you were called to bless, and the blessing comes back through your hands.
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that the inner life of the assembly is the condition of its outward witness, that you are called to bless rather than retaliate, and that the God whose eyes are on the righteous is the source of the blessing you give away.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate unity of mind, sympathy, brotherly love, a tender heart, and humility. Mortify the cold distance, the rehearsed grievance, and the sharp tongue that repays evil for evil.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Govern your tongue this week (verse 10), turn from one concrete evil (verse 11), and pursue one peace that has eluded you. And bless, specifically, the person you are tempted to curse. The priest is a conduit.</p>
