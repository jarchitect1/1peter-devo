    <h1>Christ-Centered Marital Witness</h1>
    <p class="passage">Wives and husbands, heirs together of the grace of life</p>
    <div class="lesson-no">Lesson 12 · 1 Peter 3:1–7</div>
  </div>

  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 3:1–7">
<sup class="vnum">1</sup>Likewise, wives, be subject to your own husbands, so that even if some do not obey the word, they may be won without a word by the conduct of their wives,
    <sup class="vnum">2</sup>when they see your respectful and pure conduct.
    <sup class="vnum">3</sup>Do not let your adorning be external, the braiding of hair and the putting on of gold jewelry, or the clothing you wear,
    <sup class="vnum">4</sup>but let your adorning be the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which in God's sight is very precious.
    <sup class="vnum">5</sup>For this is how the holy women who hoped in God used to adorn themselves, by submitting to their own husbands,
    <sup class="vnum">6</sup>as Sarah obeyed Abraham, calling him lord. And you are her children, if you do good and do not fear anything that is frightening.
    <sup class="vnum">7</sup>Likewise, husbands, live with your wives in an understanding way, showing honor to the woman as the weaker vessel, since they are heirs with you of the grace of life, so that your prayers may not be hindered.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">This is the third of Peter's stations, and the most intimate: the marriage. The word <em>likewise</em> ties it to the unjust master of 2:18 and the emperor of 2:13. The same Christ-shaped submission runs through every authority structure, all the way into the home. The famous verse 7, often quoted alone, sits at the end of a passage that begins with wives, and neither half makes sense without the other. Read it whole. The burdens fall on both spouses, and the goal in both cases is the same: a marriage that wins the watching world and keeps the line to heaven open.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> Wives: the witness of a quiet life</h2>

  <p>To wives, the command: <strong>"Likewise, wives, be subject to your own husbands, so that even if some do not obey the word, they may be won without a word by the conduct of their wives"</strong> (3:1). Notice Peter's first concern is not the husband's happiness; it is the <em>unbelieving husband's salvation</em>. He is writing to a specific situation, a believing wife married to a husband who "does not obey the word." In that culture, her new faith would have been socially disruptive to the household. Peter's counsel: do not nag, do not preach at him; win him by the <em>life</em>.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
Peter assumes a marriage in which the wife has come to faith and the husband has not. The submission he commands is not a doctrine of female inferiority; it is the same Christ-shaped ordering he has been teaching since 2:13, applied here with an evangelistic aim. The quietly respectful life is a sermon the unbelieving husband can read without a word.
</Aside>

  <p>And what kind of conduct wins? <strong>"Your respectful and pure conduct"</strong> (3:2). The word "respectful" (en phobō, "in fear") likely reads back to the fear of God from 2:17. Her conduct is shaped by reverence toward God, and its purity is what catches the husband's eye. He is not argued into the kingdom; he is loved there by a life he cannot explain away.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> The adorning of the heart</h2>

  <p>Then Peter presses the principle deeper, into what a wife most values: <strong>"Do not let your adorning be external, the braiding of hair and the putting on of gold jewelry, or the clothing you wear, but let your adorning be the hidden person of the heart with the imperishable beauty of a gentle and quiet spirit, which in God's sight is very precious"</strong> (3:3-4). Peter is not forbidding hair, jewelry, or clothing; he is forbidding the <em>letting</em>, the resting of a woman's identity and effort there. The real adorning is the heart.</p>

  <p>Notice the qualities: <em>gentle</em> (praus, the strength under control that Jesus claimed for Himself in Matt 11:29) and <em>quiet</em> (hēsychios, not noisy self-assertion). These are not weakness; they are the fruit of a heart at rest in God. And Peter calls this beauty <em>imperishable</em> (aphthartō), the same word used for our inheritance (1:4) and the word of God (1:23). Outer beauty fades; heart-beauty does not. In God's sight it is "very precious." Adrian Rogers lands it: God is not impressed by the wardrobe; He is captivated by the quiet trust of a heart adorned with grace (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 3:3-4</a>).</p>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> Sarah's daughters, unafraid</h2>

  <p>Peter reaches back for an example: <strong>"the holy women who hoped in God used to adorn themselves, by submitting to their own husbands, as Sarah obeyed Abraham, calling him lord"</strong> (3:5-6). The "calling him lord" is not groveling; it is Sarah's own recorded words in Genesis 18:12, said with affectionate respect. Peter holds her up as the archetype of the adorned heart.</p>

  <p>Then a striking condition: <strong>"And you are her children, if you do good and do not fear anything that is frightening"</strong> (3:6). To be Sarah's daughter is to do good <em>and</em> to be unafraid. The submission Peter honors is never cowering. It is the fearless trust of a woman whose hope is in God, so she can respect her husband without dread of what he or anyone else might do. Fearlessness and reverence belong together (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 3:6</a>).</p>

  <h2><span class="num">4.</span> Husbands: the heavier burden</h2>

  <p>Now the husbands, and the burden is heavier than is sometimes admitted: <strong>"Likewise, husbands, live with your wives in an understanding way, showing honor to the woman as the weaker vessel, since they are heirs with you of the grace of life"</strong> (3:7). Three commands fold into one.</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Live in an understanding way</strong> (kata gnōsin). The husband is to study his wife, to know her, to live with her according to knowledge, not assumptions. He is to be a student of the woman God gave him.</li>
    <li><strong>Show honor, as to the weaker vessel.</strong> "Weaker vessel" is not an insult; in the ancient world the "vessel" was an instrument, and the finer, more fragile vessel was the <em>more</em> valuable one, handled with greater care, not less. Honor is the command. She is to be prized.</li>
    <li><strong>As heirs with you of the grace of life.</strong> She is your equal heir. Whatever the ordering of the marriage, the wife is a co-heir of grace, with the same access to God and the same eternal inheritance. Honour her as such.</li>
  </ul>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The husband's submission is commanded too, in its own form: he yields his self-interest to study, prize, and honor his wife, treating her as the co-heir she is. Peter never gives one spouse a burden without giving the other a corresponding one. The marriage is Christ-shaped on both sides.
</Aside>

  <p>And then the warning that shows how seriously God takes this: <strong>"so that your prayers may not be hindered"</strong> (3:7). A husband who dishonors his wife breaks the line. His prayers are hindered. God will not be sought in the prayer closet by a man who mistreats his wife in the kitchen. David Guzik is blunt here: the way a husband treats his wife is a spiritual matter of the first order, and a barrier in the marriage becomes a barrier in heaven (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-3/">Guzik on 1 Pet 3:7</a>).</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
Christ shapes the marriage on both sides. The wife adorns the hidden heart with a gentle, quiet, fearless trust, winning even an unbelieving husband by her life. The husband studies, prizes, and honors his wife as a co-heir of grace. Both bear a burden; both are watched; both keep the line to heaven open.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
If you are married, ask your spouse one honest question this week: <em>What is one way I could honor you better?</em> Then do it. If you are not married, honor the marriages in your assembly by your speech and prayers, and pray verse 7 over every husband you know, that his prayers would not be hindered.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application — head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that marriage, for the believer, is a Christ-shaped witness on both sides. Wives are not inferior, but co-heirs of grace; husbands are not privileged, but burdened to study, prize, and honor. The marriage is a sermon to the watching world.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the gentle, quiet, fearless trust that adorns the heart, and the honouring knowledge that adorns the husband. Mortify the nagging fear, and the dismissive pride, that ruin the witness from both sides.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> If married, do one concrete act of honouring (husband) or respectful, fearless good (wife) this week, as unto the Lord. If unmarried, pray for the marriages in the assembly by name, and refuse any speech that would undermine them.</p>
