  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 1:3–5 (ESV)">
<sup class="vnum">3</sup>Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ! According to his great mercy, he has caused us to be born again to a living hope through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead,
    <sup class="vnum">4</sup>to an inheritance that is imperishable, undefiled, and unfading, kept in heaven for you,
    <sup class="vnum">5</sup>who by God's power are being guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">A letter to suffering people could have begun with a complaint, and it would have been understandable. Peter begins with a doxology instead. Before a single command falls, the air fills with blessing: <em>Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ</em>. The church in Asia Minor is anxious, slandered, and bracing for worse. And the first thing their apostle does is put a song in their mouths. That is not denial. It is priority. Peter knows that what we bless God <em>for</em> will determine how we carry what comes next.</p>

  ## <span class="num">1.</span> Mercy first, always

  <p>Notice the ground of the new birth. It is <strong>"according to his great mercy"</strong> (1:3). Not according to our merit, our repentance, our sincerity, or our progress. Mercy. The new birth is not a wage we earn; it is a gift we receive. Adrian Rogers catches the note well: salvation is "of the Lord," rooted in His pity toward people who could not rescue themselves (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 1:3</a>). Matthew Henry adds the devotional warmth: God's mercy is the fountain from which our whole hope flows, and a believer who has forgotten that fountain has forgotten the secret of his own joy (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 1:3</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The verb is active and the subject is God: <em>he has caused us to be born again</em>. We did not birth ourselves. We were born. The new life is something <em>done to us and for us</em> before it is anything we do. That single verb quietly removes every ground for pride.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">2.</span> A living hope, through a living Christ

  <p>And what are we born to? <strong>"To a living hope"</strong> (1:3). The word <a href="/en/glossary/">hope</a> here is not a wish. It is a sure expectation, an anchor. It is <em>living</em> for one reason only: it rests on a living person. <strong>"Through the resurrection of Jesus Christ from the dead."</strong> Take away the empty tomb and the hope dies with it. Keep the empty tomb and no grave can hold our future.</p>

  <p>Christian hope is unlike any other kind. Worldly hope says, <em>I hope things work out</em>, and braces for disappointment. Christian hope says, <em>Christ is risen, therefore the future is already decided</em>, and rests. The difference is not optimism. The difference is a Person who walked out of His tomb on the first day of the week.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Why &quot;living&quot;?">
A dead hope is one whose object is gone, a memory, a maybe. A living hope is one whose object is alive and present. Christ lives, so the hope lives. Our hope is as alive as He is.
</Aside>

  ## <span class="num">3.</span> An inheritance that cannot spoil

  <p>Then Peter turns to the <em>content</em> of the hope: <strong>"an inheritance that is imperishable, undefiled, and unfading, kept in heaven for you"</strong> (1:4). Three negatives, and each one knocks away a way that every earthly inheritance fails.</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Imperishable</strong> (aphtharton): it cannot decay. Moth and rust and time cannot touch it. Every earthly treasure rots; this one cannot.</li>
    <li><strong>Undefiled</strong> (amianton): it cannot be stained. Sin and sorrow cannot pollute it. The world's best gifts come polluted; this one is pure.</li>
    <li><strong>Unfading</strong> (amaranton): it cannot lose its color. Use and age cannot wear it out. Earthly joys fade the moment we taste them; this one stays bright.</li>
  </ul>

  <p>And then the doubly comforting phrase: <strong>"kept in heaven for you."</strong> Not kept in your shaking hands, but in His. Your inheritance is as safe as the throne of God. As David Guzik observes, Peter "piles up words to describe the incorruptible nature of our inheritance," and the cumulative effect is to make the believer feel how secure it is (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-1/">Guzik on 1 Pet 1:4</a>).</p>

  ## <span class="num">4.</span> Guarded, on both sides

  <p>Verse 5 completes the picture, and its grammar is a marvel of pastoral comfort. <strong>"Who by God's power are being guarded through faith for a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time."</strong> Notice the two keepings, facing each other like a pair of hands cupped around a flame:</p>

  <ul>
    <li>The inheritance is kept <strong>for you</strong> (1:4).</li>
    <li>You are kept <strong>for it</strong> (1:5).</li>
  </ul>

  <p>God's power does the keeping in both directions. The word translated "guarded" (phrouroumenous) is a military term, the picture of a garrison standing watch (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 1:5</a>; cf. <a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-1/">Guzik on 1 Pet 1:5</a>). And note that we are guarded <em>through faith</em>. God's keeping does not bypass our believing; it works <em>through</em> it. Faith is not the lock on the vault; it is the hand that receives what the vault was holding.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
Your hope is alive because Christ is alive. Your inheritance is safe because God is keeping it. And you are safe because God is keeping you, through your faith, until the day salvation is unveiled. Three securities, one mercy.
</KeyIdea>

  ## <span class="num">5.</span> Ready to be revealed

  <p>The verse ends with a final, forward turn: <strong>"a salvation ready to be revealed in the last time."</strong> Salvation here is spoken of as already ours and not yet seen. We have it. We do not yet see it. It is <em>ready</em>, waiting, like a feast prepared in the next room. The phrase "ready to be revealed" means the only thing left is the curtain. Peter is teaching these suffering believers to live between the <em>already</em> of the new birth and the <em>not yet</em> of the unveiling, and to do it with hope, not dread.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Take verses 3 to 5 and turn them into a written inventory this week. Under three headings, list what you have: <em>born again to</em> (a living hope), <em>an inheritance that is</em> (imperishable, undefiled, unfading), <em>I am</em> (guarded by God's power). Carry the list with you. When anxiety rises this week, read it before you read the news.
</Aside>

  ## Application: head, heart, hands

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that your new birth is entirely of God's mercy, that your hope is alive because Christ is risen, and that your inheritance is as secure as the throne of God. You are kept through faith, not abandoned to keep yourself.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate gratitude and security. Mortify the quiet assumption that your standing with God rises and falls with your performance. Let the <em>living</em> hope settle the anxious heart that fears the future is unwritten.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Live this week as someone whose future is already decided. Let one anxious "what if" be replaced with the calm "Christ is risen." When you meet a heavier trial, let your first words be the doxology Peter taught you: <em>Blessed be the God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ.</em></p>
