  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 5:1–4">
<sup class="vnum">1</sup>So I exhort the elders among you, as a fellow elder and a witness of the sufferings of Christ, as well as a partaker in the glory that is going to be revealed:
    <sup class="vnum">2</sup>shepherd the flock of God that is among you, exercising oversight, not under compulsion, but willingly, as God would have you; not for shameful gain, but eagerly;
    <sup class="vnum">3</sup>not domineering over those in your charge, but being examples to the flock.
    <sup class="vnum">4</sup>And when the chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the unfading crown of glory.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">Chapter five opens with Peter turning to the elders, and he comes to them not as a superior but as a <em>fellow</em> elder. The whole passage is shaped by that humility. The man who was told "feed my sheep" (John 21:17) now tells other shepherds how to feed them. The shape he gives is the opposite of the world's leadership: willing, not compelled; eager, not greedy; by example, not by domination. And over it all, holding the elders accountable and giving them hope, is the Chief Shepherd who will appear. The royal priesthood has undershepherds, and they serve in His shadow.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> A fellow elder, a witness, a partaker</h2>

  <p>Peter's credentials are striking for their meekness: <strong>"as a fellow elder and a witness of the sufferings of Christ, as well as a partaker in the glory that is going to be revealed"</strong> (5:1). He does not pull rank as an apostle. He calls himself a <em>fellow elder</em> (sympresbyteros), one of them. In a brethren assembly, where plurality of elders is the practice and servant leadership is the conviction, this is the native note. Peter stands <em>among</em> the elders, not above them.</p>

  <p>And his two credentials are the two poles of the Christian life: he is a <em>witness of the sufferings</em> and a <em>partaker in the glory</em> to be revealed. Sufferings and glory, the same pair Peter has been tracing since 1:11. Peter is not asking the elders to walk a road he has not walked. He has walked it, and he is walking it still, toward the same glory. Adrian Rogers notes the authority of earned credibility: the elder who exhorts from a shared path carries weight the elder who commands from a distance does not (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 5:1</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
Leadership in the church is exercised by those who have gone before in the path, not by those who stand aside from it. The elder's authority is the authority of a fellow pilgrim, a witness of Christ's sufferings and a partaker of His coming glory. The flock follows a shepherd who is on the same road.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> Shepherd the flock: willing, eager, by example</h2>

  <p>The main command: <strong>"shepherd the flock of God that is among you, exercising oversight"</strong> (5:2). The verb "shepherd" (poimanate) is the same word Jesus used to Peter in John 21:16, "tend my sheep." The elder's job is <a href="/en/glossary/">shepherding</a>: feeding, guiding, protecting, seeking the stray. And the flock is <em>God's</em>, not the elder's. The elder does not own the sheep; he manages them for another.</p>

  <p>Then three pairs that define <em>how</em> the shepherding is done, each a negative set against a positive:</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Not under compulsion, but willingly, as God would have you</strong> (5:2). The elder serves because he <em>wants</em> to, not because he <em>has</em> to. The willing heart is the only heart that can shepherd.</li>
    <li><strong>Not for shameful gain, but eagerly</strong> (5:2). The elder does not serve for the money or the perks. He serves <em>eagerly</em> (prothymōs), with a heart that runs ahead into the work.</li>
    <li><strong>Not domineering over those in your charge, but being examples to the flock</strong> (5:3). The elder does not lord it over the sheep. He leads by <em>example</em>, a life the flock can imitate, not a command the flock must obey.</li>
  </ul>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
The world's leadership is by compulsion, by gain, by domination. The Chief Shepherd's leadership is by willingness, by eagerness, by example. The elder who serves in His shadow cannot swap the pattern. The flock is not a workforce to be driven; it is a flock to be led by a life worth following.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> The Chief Shepherd and His crown</h2>

  <p>Now the hope that holds the elder in the work: <strong>"And when the chief Shepherd appears, you will receive the unfading crown of glory"</strong> (5:4). Notice the title: <em>Chief Shepherd</em> (archipoimenos). The elders are undershepherds; Christ is the Chief. The flock is His. The authority is His. The elders serve under Him, and to Him they will give an account (cf. Heb 13:17). This both humbles and secures them: they are not the boss, and they are not alone.</p>

  <p>And the reward: <strong>the unfading crown of glory</strong>. The word "unfading" (amarantinon) is the same root Peter used for the inheritance in 1:4, the flower that does not wither. The crown the elder receives is as durable as the inheritance of the saints. It does not fade, because it is glory shared with the Chief Shepherd Himself. Matthew Henry draws the contrast with earthly crowns: every human crown tarnishes and is taken, but the crown of the faithful undershepherd is unfading and sure (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 5:4</a>). David Guzik notes the appearing is the moment of reward, and the faithful elder's labour is not in vain (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-5/">Guzik on 1 Pet 5:4</a>).</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
The elders shepherd God's flock among them, not by compulsion, gain, or domination, but willingly, eagerly, and by example, as fellow pilgrims on the sufferings-to-glory road. They serve under the Chief Shepherd, whose appearing will bring the unfading crown of glory. The flock is His; the work is theirs; the reward is sure.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
If you are an elder, examine your shepherding against the three pairs: am I serving willingly or under compulsion? Eagerly or for gain? By example or by domination? If you are not an elder, pray for your elders by name this week, that they would serve in the Chief Shepherd's pattern, and thank God for them.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application: head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that Christ is the Chief Shepherd and the elders are His undershepherds, accountable to Him, serving His flock by His pattern. Leadership in the church is willing, eager, exemplary, never coercive or self-serving.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the willingness and eagerness that shepherd from love, not duty. Mortify the domineering spirit that drives the flock, and the mercenary spirit that serves for what it can gain. The elder leads by a life, not a lash.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> If an elder, audit your service by the three pairs and repent where you have slipped into compulsion, gain-seeking, or domination. If not an elder, honour and pray for your shepherds this week, and let your own life be an example in whatever sphere God has given you. The pattern is for every priest.</p>
