  <Scripture ref="1 Peter 5:7">
<sup class="vnum">7</sup>casting all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you.
</Scripture>

  <p class="lead opener">This is one of the best-loved verses in the New Testament, and it deserves its love. But it does not stand alone. In Greek, verse 7 is not even a full sentence; it is a participle hanging on verse 6, "humble yourselves... <em>casting</em> all your anxieties on Him." That means the casting is <em>how</em> we humble ourselves. The proud carry their own burdens because they will not trust anyone else; the humble throw theirs onto God. The verse is a single breath with two halves: a command to throw, and a reason that makes the throwing possible. Read both, because the reason is what makes the command doable.</p>

  <h2><span class="num">1.</span> Casting: the verb of a deliberate throw</h2>

  <p>The participle is vivid: <strong>"casting all your anxieties on him"</strong> (5:7). The verb (epiripsantes) means to throw something upon something else, with force and intention. It is the word used in Luke 19:35, where the people <em>threw</em> their cloaks on the colt for Jesus to sit on. It is not a careful placing or a hesitant offering. It is a deliberate heave. You pick up the anxiety and you throw it onto God.</p>

  <p>Notice that the casting is <em>all</em> your anxieties, not just the big ones or the spiritual ones. Peter does not allow a category of worries too small to cast or too worldly to cast. The whole weight, the financial worry, the relational strain, the health fear, the future dread, all of it goes onto Him. Adrian Rogers catches the picture: the Christian is not called to carry what he was never meant to carry; he is called to <em>throw</em> it (<a href="https://www.lwf.org/">Rogers, on 1 Pet 5:7</a>). The humble priest travels light because he keeps handing his loads over.</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="Notice this">
The casting is <em>how</em> we humble ourselves (verse 6). Pride says, "I can handle this." Humility says, "This is too heavy for me, and I will give it to God." Every anxiety you carry is a weight you were not made to bear. Every anxiety you cast is an act of humility, a confession that your shoulders are not divine.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">2.</span> The ground: He cares for you</h2>

  <p>Now the reason, and it is the whole gospel in five words: <strong>"because he cares for you"</strong> (5:7). The casting is not stoicism; it is not throwing your worries into a void. It is throwing them onto a <em>Person</em>, and the Person cares. The word "cares" (melei) means to have an interest in, to be concerned about, to take to heart. God is not indifferent to your life. The details that keep you awake are details He already has His eye on.</p>

  <p>This is the truth that makes the throwing possible. You will not cast your anxiety on a God you suspect does not care. But Peter, who had walked with Jesus for three years, who had watched Him notice a widow's two coins and a child's lunch and a disciple's tax bill, testifies: He cares for <em>you</em>. The God of the universe is personally attentive to your particular life. Matthew Henry draws the comfort: the care of God is not a vague providence but a fatherly attention to each of His children (<a href="https://www.biblegateway.com/resources/matthew-henry/1Peter">Henry on 1 Pet 5:7</a>). David Guzik notes the personal force of "you": the promise is not that God cares in general, but that He cares for the one casting (<a href="https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-peter-5/">Guzik on 1 Pet 5:7</a>).</p>

  <Aside type="note" title="The devotional point">
You can throw your anxiety because the One catching it cares. The casting is only as strong as the confidence in the Caretaker. Build on the second half of the verse, and the first half becomes possible. God is not a refuse heap for your worries; He is a Father whose hands are already open.
</Aside>

  <h2><span class="num">3.</span> The link to humility and to the fiery trial</h2>

  <p>Read the verse in its setting, and it deepens. Peter has just been writing about the fiery trial (4:12), the discipline at God's house (4:17), and the humbling under God's hand (5:6). These are anxious believers. They have real reasons to lose sleep. Into that, Peter does not say "stop worrying" as a bare command; he says "throw it onto the God who cares." The humility of 5:6 and the casting of 5:7 are the one response of the suffering priest to his circumstances: he goes low, and he lets go. He cannot control the fire, but he can throw its weight onto God.</p>

  <p>And this verse, quietly, answers the temptation to self-reliance that suffering produces. The believer under pressure is tempted to <em>white-knuckle</em> it, to prove his faith by carrying the load heroically. Peter says that is not faith; that is pride. Faith throws. The priest who humbles himself is the priest who casts, because both are the same act: the act of admitting that God is God and I am not, and that His shoulders, not mine, were made for this weight.</p>

  <KeyIdea label="The single takeaway">
Cast all your anxieties on Him, because He cares for you. The throwing is how you humble yourself under God's hand; the throwing is possible because the One catching your load is a Father whose hands are open. The priest who goes low is the priest who lets go.
</KeyIdea>

  <Aside type="note" title="Try this">
Take a piece of paper and write down the anxieties you are carrying right now, as many as come. Then, physically, do what the verse says: read each one, and pray, "I throw this on You, because You care for me." Crumple the paper if it helps. Let the casting be a real act, not a vague feeling.
</Aside>

  <h2>Application — head, heart, hands</h2>

  <p><strong>Head.</strong> Believe that God cares for you, not in a vague providential way but personally, and that the casting of anxiety is how you humble yourself under His hand. You were never made to carry what only God can carry.</p>

  <p><strong>Heart.</strong> Cultivate the trust that throws rather than grips. Mortify the self-reliance that white-knuckles through suffering to prove its strength, and the suspicion that God is indifferent to your details. The Caretaker cares.</p>

  <p><strong>Hands.</strong> Cast. Take the anxieties you have been carrying, name them, and throw them onto God in deliberate prayer. Make it a daily, even hourly, act. The humble priest travels light because he keeps handing his loads over to the God whose hands are open.</p>
